## Preface

This book was written in Q2 2026, when production AI systems had been running long enough to accumulate a documented record of failure, and the tooling built to observe them had been running long enough to show where it holds and where it does not. Both records are specific. This book is a structured reading of both.

**What this book is.** An analytical account of production AI observability at a specific point in time, organized by the engineering disciplines that will survive tool generations and the product-state facts that will not. The book argues that the observability gap created by AI workloads is structural rather than configurational, that closing it requires a combination of principled engineering discipline and Datadog-specific implementation knowledge, and that the teams navigating this gap most effectively are those who have distinguished between what they need to know in principle and what they need to verify against current documentation.

The argument advances through five parts. Part I maps the gap: what AI workloads require that current observability practice does not provide, and why the gap is specific rather than general. Part II establishes the engineering principles that apply regardless of which tools or platforms a team uses: cardinality discipline, tracing for non-deterministic systems, and the epistemological separation between operational monitoring and evaluation monitoring. Part III documents what happens when these principles are not applied: a catalog of AI-era production incidents through Q2 2026, organized by failure category with analysis of what monitoring would have caught each one earlier. Part IV examines Datadog's specific product surface in detail, with assessment of what it covers, where it stops, and how to instrument it correctly. Part V assesses where the trajectory leads, including the recursive governance problem that arises when the system doing anomaly detection is itself an AI system.

**What this book is not.** A guide to getting started with AI systems. A product review of Datadog versus competitors. A comprehensive catalog of every LLM observability tool on the market. A playbook for teams that have not yet shipped an AI-backed system and want a blueprint for doing so.

This book assumes the reader has already shipped an LLM-backed system, or is about to. The reader knows what a span is. The reader knows what a token is. The reader knows what an agent is. The book does not define these terms. It opens at the gap and proceeds analytically.

**Who this book is for.** Senior platform engineers, SREs, and observability practitioners who are operating LLM-backed systems in production and have encountered the limits of their existing monitoring practice. ML engineers and AI engineers who are trying to understand what the platform team needs from them and vice versa. Engineering managers who are trying to make defensible procurement decisions about observability tooling for AI workloads and need an analytical frame for the decision, not a vendor comparison matrix.

The book is also for the reader who has read the vendor documentation, the DASH talks, the research reports, and the incident retrospectives and wants a single document that synthesizes them into a coherent analytical account. That synthesis is what this book attempts.

**What the book assumes the reader knows.** Distributed tracing fundamentals: what a span is, what a trace is, what parent-child relationships mean, what context propagation requires. Datadog basics: APM, custom metrics, log management, and the general shape of the Datadog product surface. LLM fundamentals: what a token is, what a context window is, what a completion is, what an agent is in the LLM sense. The reader does not need to know Datadog's LLM Observability product specifically; Part IV provides that treatment. The reader does not need to have read the OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions; Chapters 4 and 8 provide the relevant treatment.

**The dated-snapshot framing.** This book has a dateline: Q2 2026. That dateline is not a hedge. It is an analytical tool. The book distinguishes explicitly between claims grounded in mathematical properties and epistemological distinctions that will outlast the current product generation, and claims grounded in specific product versions, billing figures, and competitive capability assessments that will not. Chapter 12 applies what the book calls the Lindy filter to this distinction systematically, assessing which of the book's own claims are likely to hold in five years and which require re-verification before acting on them.

The verification-date convention noted on the copyright page applies throughout Part IV. The notation signals which claims are product-state snapshots that readers should verify against current documentation before making engineering or procurement decisions. A billing model can change in a product release. The epistemological boundary between operational monitoring and evaluation monitoring does not change with a product release.

**The Lindy filter convention.** The Lindy filter, introduced as a named concept in Chapter 12 but operative throughout the book's structure, is the heuristic that knowledge surviving multiple technology transitions is more likely to survive the next one than knowledge that depends on a specific product version or vendor roadmap announcement. This book is structured with that filter in mind: the principle-level chapters are written to remain useful beyond Q2 2026; the product-level chapters are written to be accurate at Q2 2026 and are explicitly flagged for time-sensitive verification.

Readers who approach the book as a complete technical reference, expecting every claim to be current in perpetuity, will be disappointed. Readers who approach it as an analytical artifact with explicit temporal framing, using the principle-level chapters as durable reference and the product-level chapters as a Q2 2026 baseline to update from, will find it more useful than any document that does not mark its own expiration dates.

**A note on what this book became.** This book was assembled from a synthesis of public research reports, Datadog documentation, DASH talk recordings, independent cost analyses, and documented incident retrospectives. The analytical framework, the named patterns, and the structural argument are original to this work. The facts are drawn from the cited public corpus. No fact in this book is fabricated; every specific date, dollar amount, named incident, named person, and product capability claim is traceable to a cited source.

The twelve chapters accumulated, in sequence, a vocabulary for talking about production AI observability precisely: named patterns for the failure modes (the Cardinality Cliff, the Span Topology Problem, the Eval/Ops Split, the Six Incident Categories), named patterns for the engineering disciplines (Token-Cost Attribution, the Context Propagation Contract, Autonomous Monitoring Governance), and named patterns for the analytical frames (the Lindy Filter, the Platform Stack vs the Specialized Stack). The glossary at the back of the book catalogs these patterns for reference.

The book's overall verdict on production AI observability at Q2 2026 is this: the tools exist, the principles are articulable, the failure modes are documented, and the gap between having these resources and using them correctly is still large enough that most teams are discovering it in production rather than at design time. This book is an attempt to move that discovery earlier.

## How to Read This Book

**The five-part structure.** The book is organized as five parts, twelve chapters, each part building on the previous one without restating it.

Part I (Chapters 1 and 2) maps the territory: the specific dimensions of the observability gap that AI workloads create, and the new component classes that generative AI systems introduce as observable entities. These chapters establish the named patterns and the analytical vocabulary that the rest of the book uses as shorthand.

Part II (Chapters 3, 4, and 5) establishes the engineering principles that apply regardless of which tools or platforms a team uses. Cardinality and sampling are Chapter 3's territory; distributed tracing for non-deterministic systems is Chapter 4's; the epistemological separation between operational monitoring and evaluation monitoring is Chapter 5's. These chapters are the most durable in the book by the Lindy filter assessment.

Part III (Chapter 6) is the incident catalog: documented AI-era production failures through Q2 2026, organized by failure category with analysis of what monitoring discipline would have caught each one earlier. This chapter reads best as a practitioner's case library, consulted when a specific failure category becomes relevant.

Part IV (Chapters 7, 8, 9, and 10) is the product-level treatment: Datadog's specific AI surface, instrumentation in practice, cost engineering, and the competitive landscape. These chapters are the most time-sensitive in the book. Claims in Part IV are dated to Q2 2026 and should be verified against current documentation before any engineering or procurement decision depends on them.

Part V (Chapters 11 and 12) looks at where the trajectory leads. Chapter 11 treats autonomous monitoring and the governance problem that arises when the anomaly detection system is itself an AI system. Chapter 12 applies the Lindy filter to the book's own contents, distinguishing what is likely to remain true from what is likely to require revision.

**Reading sequentially.** The book is designed to be read sequentially. Each chapter builds on the named patterns established in earlier chapters, using bolded shorthand names rather than re-explaining the concepts. A reader who skips Part II and goes directly to Part IV will encounter shorthand references to the Cardinality Cliff, the Eval/Ops Split, and the Span Topology Problem without the analytical grounding those chapters provide. The shorthand is deliberate: the book treats the reader as someone working through the argument, not consulting a reference.

**Reading selectively.** Practitioners who are already fluent in the principle-level material can start at Chapter 7 and use Part IV as the practical reference it is designed to be, with occasional references back to the glossary for pattern definitions. Chapter 6 reads as a standalone incident catalog for teams doing incident analysis or preparing runbooks. Chapter 12 reads as a standalone durability assessment for practitioners deciding which claims from any AI observability resource are worth internalizing versus treating as time-stamped snapshots.

**The dated-snapshot caveat.** The Q2 2026 dateline is explicit and intentional. Product capabilities, billing models, coverage matrices, and competitive landscape assessments are snapshots. The verification-date notation "(per Datadog documentation, accessed Q2 2026)" signals which claims require re-verification. The Lindy filter assessment in Chapter 12 identifies the tier each category of claim inhabits: stable, medium-cadence, or fast-moving.

**No diagrams.** This book contains no diagrams, figures, charts, or inline images. All architectures, topologies, data flows, and system relationships are described in structured prose. This is intentional, not an omission. Every architecture description names components, states directional relationships, traces data flow, and specifies boundaries explicitly in text. Every span topology is traced step by step in prose. Every competitive comparison uses prose paragraphs or tables (maximum four columns). The reader who needs a visual aid is encouraged to sketch one from the prose description; the prose provides sufficient specificity to do so.

**The named-pattern convention.** Each chapter introduces one to three named patterns, formatted in bold when they first appear and given a brief callout block definition. Subsequent references within the chapter and across the book use the bolded name as shorthand. The glossary at the back of the book provides an alphabetical catalog of all named patterns with the chapter that introduces each one.
