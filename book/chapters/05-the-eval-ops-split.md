## Chapter 5. Operational Metrics vs. Evaluation Metrics: Two Disciplines That Must Not Collapse

In early 2025, the platform engineering team at a mid-size financial services company received an urgent escalation from the product team responsible for the company's LLM-backed wealth advisory chatbot. Customer satisfaction scores for the chatbot had declined sharply over three weeks. The product team's internal quality tracking showed that the fraction of conversations ending with a user thumbs-down had climbed from approximately 12 percent to over 28 percent across the same period.

The platform team opened Datadog. What they found there was confounding. P95 latency: 4.2 seconds, well within the configured SLO of 8 seconds. Error rate on the LLM spans: 0.3 percent, within tolerance. Token consumption per session: slightly elevated but within expected range. Infrastructure health: green across every dimension. From every signal the platform team knew how to read, the system was operating correctly. The dashboards told a story of a healthy, performant service.

The product team, running a parallel track, had been collecting something different. A domain expert from the wealth advisory group had spent two weeks reviewing sampled conversations, scoring outputs for factual accuracy, appropriate product citations, and regulatory compliance. Those scores told a completely different story. The fraction of responses citing the wrong fund products had tripled. The fraction of responses containing outdated yield figures had nearly doubled. The model was producing answers that were confident, grammatically correct, well-structured, and wrong in ways that could expose the firm to regulatory liability. The LLM had not broken. It had drifted. The drift was invisible to every operational tool the platform team had in place.

The two teams spent another two weeks investigating in parallel before a joint postmortem produced an insight that should have been established at system design time: they were asking different questions, using different methods, producing incompatible results, and routing those incompatible findings to the same group of people who had no framework for adjudicating between them. One team was asking whether the system was healthy. The other was asking whether the model was performing well. These are different questions. They have different answers. In early 2025, neither team had a name for the distinction between them, and that absence of a name was, structurally, the problem.

---

What makes that wealth advisory team's situation relevant to this chapter is not the drift itself. Model drift is a known phenomenon, documented in the academic literature and in practitioner accounts stretching back to the pre-LLM era of ML deployment. The relevant point is that the two teams' parallel investigation represented a structural confusion that a well-designed system would have prevented: the conflation of two fundamentally distinct measurement disciplines, each with its own questions, methods, toolchains, and ownership patterns. That conflation imposed two weeks of wasted investigative effort on a production incident. It produced a postmortem that blamed monitoring inadequacy when the actual failure was organizational: the wrong team was looking at the wrong signals for the wrong problem. And it reproduced a failure pattern that the research corpus documents repeatedly across production AI deployments from 2024 through Q2 2026.

This chapter names that distinction, argues for why it matters more in AI systems than in traditional services, and lays out precisely where the two disciplines overlap and where they diverge. The Datadog-specific instrumentation surfaces where each discipline lives in practice are treated in Part IV. This chapter establishes the principle-level boundary that makes those implementation choices meaningful.

---

**The Eval/Ops Split.** The principled separation between production observability (is the system healthy?) and LLM evaluation (is the model performing well?), two disciplines that answer different questions, require different methods, must not be conflated in toolchain design or team ownership, and cannot be resolved by running more of either discipline's tools on the other discipline's questions. The Eval/Ops Split is not a claim about which discipline matters more. It is a claim about what each discipline can and cannot see, and about the organizational and toolchain consequences of treating them as a single coherent practice when they are not.

---

### Two Questions, Two Methods

The operational question, "is the system healthy?", has a long and well-developed answer in the observability literature. It asks about latency distributions, error rates, availability percentages, resource utilization, and dependency health. These signals are mechanical. A span either closes with a 200 status or it does not. A P99 latency is either within the SLO threshold or it is not. A pod either has available CPU or it does not. The signal is binary or continuous in a mechanically deterministic way. The measurement apparatus, a tracing library, a metrics collector, a log ingestion pipeline, records physical facts about the system's computational behavior. Given the same measurement event, the same apparatus will produce the same reading. The determinism is built into the instrument.

The evaluation question, "is the model performing well?", is structurally different in kind. It asks about semantic properties of model outputs: factual accuracy, logical consistency, appropriate tone, relevance to the user's actual intent, absence of harmful content, groundedness in the retrieved context, adherence to safety policy. These are not mechanical properties. A response's factual accuracy cannot be determined by reading a status code or measuring a duration. It requires comparison against an external standard of truth, which may itself be probabilistic, context-dependent, and contested. The measurement apparatus for evaluation is not a tracing library. It is a judge, human or LLM-based, applying rubrics developed by domain experts, calibrated against human rater agreement, and subject to its own drift over time.

This difference in measurement method has a direct implication for what counts as a valid signal. An operational metric carries an inherent guarantee of determinism: every measurement of the same event will produce the same result given the same measurement apparatus. A latency of 4.2 seconds, measured by two different tracing libraries implementing the same specification, will agree within instrumentation error margins that are fractions of a millisecond. An LLM evaluation score, produced by two different judges evaluating the same response, may disagree substantially even when both judges are well-calibrated. Phillip Carter, Principal PM at Honeycomb, documented this concretely in the published account of Honeycomb's Query Assistant SLO design: after multiple iterations of calibrating the LLM judge against human raters, the team achieved greater than 90 percent agreement, which they treated as acceptable for their context but which is still materially worse than the determinism of a mechanical measurement, and worse by the factor that matters most for alerting policy design (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Carter's published account).

The implication is that conflating operational metrics with evaluation metrics does not simply combine two kinds of signals in one place. It combines two fundamentally different epistemologies in a single dashboard or alert policy, and that conflation produces confusion in both directions.

The epistemological difference has a practical consequence for how each discipline's signals should be used in incident response. Operational signals are designed to be actionable immediately. A P99 latency alert that fires at 2 a.m. carries an implicit claim that the system is in a state that requires intervention now, and the SRE who receives it can verify or refute that claim within minutes using the trace backend. Evaluation signals are designed to be actionable over a longer window. A decline in hallucination rate over a seven-day rolling average carries an implicit claim that the model's behavior has shifted in a way that requires investigation by the coalition of domain experts, ML engineers, and product managers who together own the quality rubric. The SRE who receives that signal at 2 a.m. cannot verify or refute it without the domain expertise and evaluation toolchain that the coalition maintains. The signal is correct and urgent, but it is not operationally urgent. The appropriate response is investigation the following business day, not immediate incident escalation.

This is why the most experienced practitioners in the research corpus, including Carter at Honeycomb, Parker at Honeycomb, and the Applied-LLMs collective, have converged on the pattern of routing evaluation-layer alerts to low-urgency channels rather than to incident response queues. The routing decision is not a statement about the importance of quality. It is a statement about who can respond to a quality alert with useful action at 2 a.m. The answer, consistently, is nobody with the right context. The Eval/Ops Split is, among other things, a routing discipline.

When an SRE team is paged on a quality regression signaled through the operational channel, they arrive at the incident with the wrong tools. They know how to look at traces, examine latency distributions, check error rates, and correlate infrastructure anomalies with application behavior. They do not have the domain expertise to judge whether a particular response to a wealth advisory question contained an outdated yield figure. They do not have access to the ground-truth dataset that would allow them to score factual accuracy. They cannot determine from a Datadog trace whether the retrieved documents were relevant to the query. The alert reached the right toolchain. It reached the wrong team, equipped with the wrong methods, for the wrong problem.

The reverse failure is equally common and equally costly. When a machine learning or product team investigating a suspected quality regression focuses exclusively on model output scores without checking operational metrics, they may spend weeks chasing a quality degradation whose proximate cause is an operational change: a retrieval configuration update that changed which documents were being injected into the prompt context, an embedding model update that quietly altered the coordinate space of the vector index, a context-length limit that began truncating the system prompt under higher-than-expected load, a token budget change that caused the model to produce shorter responses. All of these operational changes produce quality-looking regressions in the evaluation layer. None of them are findable by examining evaluation scores alone. The model has not changed. The pipeline has. The evaluation team chases the wrong signal. The resolution requires looking at the operational telemetry that the evaluation toolchain does not display.

### The Overlap: Where Both Disciplines Converge

The Eval/Ops Split is not a claim that operational signals and evaluation signals have no relationship. There is a band of signals where they converge, and understanding that band is as important as understanding where they diverge. The disciplines share evidence at the edges. They do not share methods or epistemological standing anywhere in the shared zone; they simply happen to measure phenomena that are simultaneously mechanical and semantic.

Latency is the most obvious point of convergence. An LLM that takes 30 seconds to respond to a simple factual query is both operationally degraded (the latency SLO is violated) and, in a qualified sense, evaluatively degraded (the response may have arrived too late to be useful, which is a form of quality failure regardless of the response's semantic content). Time-to-first-token matters for user experience in ways that are simultaneously operational and qualitative. Bloomberg's multi-cluster generative AI inference platform, documented in a series of KubeCon talks by Alexa Griffith and Sal Furino, explicitly treats time-to-first-token, tokens per output token, token throughput, and queue depth as first-class SLO dimensions alongside the standard latency and error-rate metrics, treating the serving-side performance properties of LLM inference as continuous with the operational health of the surrounding platform (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Bloomberg's KubeCon talk series). At the latency extremes, the two disciplines agree: a response that never arrives is both an operational error and an evaluation failure, and the distinction between disciplines is not operationally meaningful when the failure is that complete.

Error rates carry the same dual character. A hard failure in the LLM API call, a 429 rate limit or a 503 provider error, is an operational event recorded by the trace. It is also an evaluation event in the sense that the system produced no useful output for the user. Hard failures appear in both the operational error rate and, if the evaluation pipeline is properly instrumented, as a distinct event class in the evaluation log. Teams that maintain this dual recording can distinguish between "the system failed to respond at all" and "the system responded with content that was factually incorrect," which are meaningfully different failure categories even though both register as a quality regression from the user's perspective.

Structural correctness is the most actionable convergence zone. When an LLM system is designed to produce structured output, a JSON object, a YAML configuration block, a SQL query, a function call with typed parameters, the output's structural validity is mechanically determinable. A JSON parse failure is an operational event. Schema validation failure is deterministic. Whether the parsed JSON contains semantically correct values is an evaluation question, but whether it parses at all is not. Phillip Carter's SLO design for Honeycomb's Query Assistant formalized this insight as the first line of SLI design: define any failure in the set of API call, output parse, schema validate, and downstream query execution as a boolean false, and build the SLO on top of those boolean outcomes (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Carter's published account). This converts the non-deterministic question of "was the query good?" into the deterministic sub-question of "did the query parse and execute without error?" The pattern generalizes. LinkedIn, documented in the research corpus, constrains its routing LLM to emit YAML and treats schema-validation failures as deterministic error events, converting a model-quality signal into a mechanical signal that can support a traditional SLO (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026). Shopify's Global Catalogue routes outputs with low joint log-probability to human review rather than serving them as responses, treating model uncertainty as a structural correctness signal (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026).

The structural correctness layer belongs to both disciplines simultaneously, and it is the most defensible layer for operational alerting on AI systems. It does not require a judge. It does not require calibration against human raters. It requires only a deterministic boundary that the model's output must cross: parse, validate, execute. Everything that crosses the boundary is operationally successful. Everything that fails the boundary is operationally failed. The semantic quality of outputs that cross the boundary is an evaluation question, addressed by the evaluation toolchain, owned by the evaluation coalition, and answered on evaluation timescales.

### The Divergence: Where the Disciplines Separate

Beyond the overlap band, the two disciplines diverge in ways that make their conflation structurally costly.

**Hallucination and factual accuracy** live entirely in the evaluation domain. There is no operational signal for a hallucination. A response that confidently asserts a fictional regulatory requirement, or cites a fund's five-year return using last year's figures, or attributes a quote to a person who never said it, will produce an HTTP 200 status, a successful span, a token count within the normal range, and a latency within the SLO. The operational toolchain records a successful transaction. The trace shows a healthy interaction. The hallucination is invisible until a human or a properly calibrated evaluator reads the output and compares it against a source of truth.

The academic literature on this structural limitation is unambiguous. Two 2024 papers establish from different theoretical frameworks that hallucination rates in LLM systems cannot be driven to zero by construction: Banerjee et al. (arXiv:2409.05746) argues from learning theory that any model trained on finite data will have non-zero probability of generating false statements, and Xu et al. (arXiv:2401.11817) argues from undecidability theory that the ground-truth oracle required to catch all hallucinations is computationally intractable (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026). The implication for SLO design is that a zero-error-budget policy for hallucination is not a tractable operational target. The practitioner community has absorbed this finding and operationalized it. Applied-LLMs, the collective of practitioners that includes Eugene Yan, Hamel Husain, Jason Liu, Shreya Shankar, Bryan Bischof, and Charles Frye, reports the observed production floor as a 5 to 10 percent baseline hallucination rate, with a hard floor around 2 percent even on simple summarization tasks, citing LLM provider data (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026).

Honeycomb's Query Assistant is the most thoroughly published public case of a team confronting this reality explicitly. The initial success-rate target was 75 percent, with Carter's account making explicit that "most services have a 95% or higher success target, but we felt that didn't make sense for the initial release of a feature using an LLM." His operational rule of thumb for teams starting an LLM quality SLO: "Start with 50% or higher as your success rate, but don't go higher than 90%" (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Carter's published account). That target reflects an accurate description of what the measurement can support, not a failure of ambition. The error budget for a quality SLO is structurally one to two orders of magnitude looser than for an availability SLO. A team that attempts to apply traditional web-service SLO targets to LLM quality metrics will find that the SLO fires constantly, becomes noise, and gets ignored. The lesson is not to set looser availability SLOs for AI services. It is to accept that the quality SLO lives in a different regime with different budget expectations, owned by a different team, answered by different methods.

**Toxicity, tone, and safety policy compliance** are similarly evaluation-only territory. A response that violates the company's content policy produces the same operational trace as a response that complies with it. Automated evaluators, specifically models fine-tuned or prompted to detect harmful content, bias, or policy violations, are the only toolchain that can flag these. Datadog ships out-of-the-box evaluation templates for toxicity, sentiment, and prompt injection as managed evaluators in LLM Observability (Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026). These templates run as automated LLM-as-judge evaluations against sampled production traces. The results flow into the LLM Observability dashboard as scored events alongside the operational trace data. The functional gap is that Datadog does not publish opinionated targets for these scores: the metric exists; the policy for what constitutes an acceptable toxicity score or hallucination rate is left to the operator. This is consistent with the broader community posture documented in the research corpus. No vendor publishes a quantitative industry-consensus target for a hallucination or groundedness rate at the SLO level. The instrumentation is maturing faster than the policy framework around it.

**Retrieval quality** sits at the operational-evaluation boundary in a way that illuminates the Split precisely. Whether retrieved documents are semantically relevant to the query is an evaluation question. Whether the retrieval call succeeded, returned the configured number of results, and completed within the latency budget are operational questions. A team that monitors only the operational signals from the retrieval layer knows whether the call succeeded. It does not know whether the documents it returned were the right documents. This is the failure mode that the financial services team described in Chapter 2 encountered: the retrieval layer was operationally healthy while producing semantically incorrect results. The **Retrieval Layer**, introduced in Chapter 2 as the subsystem within a RAG pipeline that fetches document chunks and injects them into the prompt context, sits on exactly this operational-evaluation boundary. Its latency, error rate, and response code are operational signals. Its relevance scores, its document freshness, and its semantic appropriateness to the query are evaluation signals. The two signal types require different instruments, different ownership, and different response playbooks.

**Distribution drift** occupies a middle zone that the community has increasingly brought into the evaluation toolchain. Maria Vechtomova, speaking at SRECon EMEA 2025, articulated the core insight: "The statistical properties of the data can change, and your model stops performing. That's what happened during COVID: forecasting and recommender systems broke because we had never seen that kind of data before" (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Vechtomova's SRECon EMEA 2025 talk). Distribution drift alerts on the absence of evidence, on the divergence of the current query distribution from the historical baseline, rather than on the presence of explicit failures. WhyLabs built its product architecture around whylogs-based drift profiles. Arize Phoenix provides embedding drift visualization. Dynatrace categorizes model drift and data drift as named observability concerns alongside standard operational metrics. This drift detection work is evaluation-adjacent: it does not measure the quality of individual responses, but it provides early warning that the system is operating in territory where its calibrated behavior may no longer apply. It is the leading indicator that the evaluation coalition needs to see before the evaluation scores themselves reveal the regression.

**Service drift** is a closely related phenomenon documented in the recent academic literature (PMC12863567, 2025; longitudinal LLM-judge benchmarks). Hosted LLM providers make silent updates to the model behind a stable API endpoint. The provider does not announce the update. The model identifier in the span remains unchanged. The operational telemetry shows no disruption. The evaluation scores begin to drift because the model's behavior has shifted in ways that are not reflected in any operational signal. The appropriate monitoring response, pinning model versions where the provider supports it and treating unannounced behavior shifts detected through evaluation-score drift as an alert condition, is an evaluation-layer practice with no operational-layer analog. It requires the evaluation coalition to be watching the evaluation signals independently of whatever the operational toolchain is showing.

### The Judge Reliability Problem

The evaluation discipline has its own version of the measurement reliability challenge, one that is distinct from both the hallucination problem and the distribution drift problem and that the operational discipline does not face. The judge, the LLM-as-judge evaluator that scores production responses against a rubric, is itself a non-deterministic system whose reliability must be monitored and maintained. An LLM-as-judge that was calibrated against human raters in January 2025 will, by June 2025, have drifted in its rubric application in ways that may not be visible without re-calibration. The rubric drift problem is documented empirically: a 2026 paper titled "Rubrics as an Attack Surface" (arXiv:2602.13576) demonstrated that harmlessness accuracy in a calibrated judge dropped 27.9 percent under adversarial pressure, without any change to the judge's surface-level behavior (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026). A second 2025 paper from Wells Fargo researchers, "Who Judges the Judge?" (arXiv:2512.01786), documented that individual LLM judges hallucinate their own evaluations roughly one in ten times, producing a noise floor in any single-judge SLO that cannot be reduced by the operational techniques of lowering alert thresholds or increasing metric sampling frequency (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026).

These findings have a direct implication for teams building evaluation-layer SLOs. A quality SLO built on the output of a single, uncalibrated judge is not a stable measurement instrument. It is a system with its own drift characteristics and its own failure modes. The meta-problem of monitoring the judge is structurally the same as the problem of monitoring the model: both are non-deterministic systems whose behavior can change without producing operational signals.

The practitioner community has developed three mitigations, none of which fully resolves the problem. First, quorum-of-judges approaches, exemplified by the Wells Fargo "LLM Jury-on-Demand" paper, run multiple judges on the same response and require agreement before treating a low score as an alert condition (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026). This reduces noise at the cost of latency and inference expense. Second, periodic human-anchored re-calibration, with the calibration interval informed by the rate of model drift in the judge itself, provides a periodic ground-truth reset. The Applied-LLMs collective explicitly recommends "look at samples every day" as part of on-call rotations, using what they call the "intern test": if a human intern in the relevant domain could grade the output given the same context, the task is fair to evaluate, and a sampled human review pipeline provides the ground truth against which the judge is calibrated (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026). Third, score smoothing, running multiple evaluations on the same response and taking the average or the majority vote, reduces the impact of individual judge responses that fall outside the judge's calibrated distribution.

The upshot is that the evaluation discipline requires more maintenance than the operational discipline. A latency SLO, once configured correctly, continues to fire correctly indefinitely without recalibration. A quality SLO requires ongoing calibration work to remain a reliable measurement. Teams that treat the evaluation layer as a one-time configuration, setting up a judge template and then relying on it as a stable instrument, will find that the SLO's reliability degrades over time in ways that are difficult to detect without explicit monitoring of the judge itself. This is not a reason to avoid evaluation-layer SLOs. It is a reason to staff and operate them with the understanding that the measurement apparatus requires the same ongoing investment as the system it is measuring.

### The SLO Problem for Non-Deterministic Systems

The SLO framework, as developed by the Google Site Reliability Engineering group and formalized in the SRE Workbook, was designed for systems whose error budget can be defined against a binary oracle: a request either succeeded or it failed, and the budget tracks the fraction of failures over a rolling window. The burn-rate alert fires when the failure fraction is consuming the budget faster than the window can replenish it. This framework is highly functional for operational metrics, where the oracle is mechanical and the failure definition is unambiguous.

For evaluation metrics, the oracle problem is not mechanical. There is no ground truth available in real time for most quality signals. The system cannot know, at the moment of serving a response, whether that response is factually accurate without running a separate evaluation that is itself probabilistic. The burn-rate alert framework, applied directly to evaluation scores, must therefore be applied to an SLI that is itself a sample, produced by a non-deterministic judge, with measurement error that is one to two orders of magnitude larger than a mechanical failure count.

The community's response to this situation is instructive, because it has converged on a practical decomposition rather than waiting for a theoretical resolution. The defensible pattern, drawn from the most concrete published cases, separates the SLO into two layers. The first layer applies burn-rate alerting only to the deterministic sub-checks: schema validation failures, downstream execution errors, hard API failures, structural correctness. These have real boolean signals and can support a traditional error-budget framework with normal alert thresholds and escalation policies. The second layer applies anomaly detection and distribution monitoring to the evaluation scores, watching for sustained directional drift over windows long enough to absorb the noise in individual judge evaluations, rather than firing alerts on individual score violations.

Phillip Carter's design for Honeycomb's Query Assistant used a seven-day SLO window with a Slack notification four hours before budget exhaustion, and explicitly rejected PagerDuty alerting for LLM quality failures on the grounds that a rogue user attempting prompt injection should not wake someone up at 2 a.m. (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Carter's published account). The distinction between a Slack notification and a PagerDuty page is not merely about notification channel. It reflects a principled decision about which failure modes require immediate human intervention and which require investigation on the next business day. Operational failures, a provider outage, a latency spike above the configured ceiling, a hard error rate exceeding the budget, require immediate response. Quality regressions, a gradual drift in hallucination rate, a trend toward lower relevance scores in a specific query category, require investigation but not interruption. The alert policy encodes the discipline's epistemological standing.

Austin Parker of Honeycomb extended the burn-rate framework to agentic systems in an April 2026 post that is among the most practically grounded accounts of agentic SLO design in the public record: "If an agent is making code changes that technically pass tests but cause a gradual SLO burn, you need that burn rate alert to fire and trigger a review. The agent doesn't need to know it's drifting; your observability platform does" (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Parker's April 2026 Honeycomb post). The "gradual SLO burn" Parker describes is a burn-rate alert applied to the structural correctness layer. The tests that pass are the evaluation layer's deterministic sub-checks. The drift that the observability platform detects is distribution drift in the evaluation signals. All three operate simultaneously, but they are organized and owned differently.

Denys Vasyliev's framing in "AI Reliability Engineering: Welcome to the Third Age of SRE," published in The New Stack in June 2025, captures the conceptual reframe that the Eval/Ops Split requires: "Unlike crashing pods or failing endpoints, silent model degradation slips under the radar. The model keeps responding, but its answers grow weaker, biased, or just wrong. Users don't see 500 errors; they get hallucinations, toxic outputs, or faulty decisions. In the world of AI, correctness is uptime. When reliability means quality, degradation is downtime" (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Vasyliev's June 2025 article). The reframe does not collapse the two disciplines. It redefines what each discipline's failure mode looks like in the AI context, and it assigns "correctness" to the evaluation discipline rather than to the operational discipline, precisely because correctness cannot be measured mechanically.

The EU AI Act brings a regulatory dimension to this framework that is material for teams deploying AI in regulated industries. Articles 9 and 17 of the Act require providers of high-risk AI systems, as defined in Annex III, to establish risk management systems and implement a post-market monitoring plan that tracks the system's performance throughout its lifecycle (EU AI Act, Articles 9 and 17, in force August 2024). The monitoring obligation extends beyond uptime and error rates to include the system's ability to perform its intended purpose accurately. The Act does not prescribe specific technical standards for what evaluation monitoring must include; the EU AI Office's implementation guidance was still in development as of Q2 2026. But the obligation creates a legal context in which the Eval/Ops Split is not merely a best-practice distinction. For systems classified as high-risk under Annex III, which includes AI systems used in critical infrastructure, educational qualification, employment decisions, essential services access, law enforcement, migration management, administration of justice, and democratic processes, the post-market monitoring plan must address evaluation signals alongside operational signals. A compliance team that attempts to satisfy this obligation using only the operational monitoring layer is not meeting the Act's intent, even if the Datadog dashboard shows green.

### The Ownership Problem

The Eval/Ops Split has an organizational analog that is, in practice, harder to resolve than the toolchain separation. Operational metrics are owned by the SRE or platform team. The SRE team has the context, the toolchain access, and the on-call rotation to respond to operational alerts. Evaluation metrics are owned, in the published accounts of the teams that have solved this problem well, by a coalition: domain experts who define the rubric, ML engineers who implement and calibrate the judge, and product teams who set the acceptable quality thresholds.

This coalition does not have a PagerDuty rotation. It does not have an incident response playbook in the SRE sense. The work the coalition does is not incident response work. It is continuous improvement work: reviewing sampled outputs, refining rubrics, calibrating judges against human rater agreement, tracking quality trends over weeks rather than minutes. Hamel Husain's field guide to LLM evaluation, widely cited in the practitioner community, states the ownership principle directly: "Empower domain experts to write prompts and define eval criteria" (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Husain's published field guide). Carter's Honeycomb account is the clearest case study: a Principal Product Manager wrote the Query Assistant prompts, designed the user-feedback schema, calibrated the LLM judge through three iterations, and reached more than 90 percent agreement with human raters. An SRE did not own that work. A machine learning engineer did not own that work. The domain expert who understood what a correct query assistant response looked like owned it.

The research corpus documents three specific ownership models that have been shown to work at different organizational scales, and it documents a failure mode that appears consistently when none of them are in place.

The PM-led model, exemplified by Honeycomb, works at small-to-medium scale where the product manager has direct domain expertise and technical literacy. The PM owns the rubric, the feedback schema, and the judge calibration. SRE owns the burn-rate alert infrastructure. ML engineering provides toolchain support. This model breaks down when the domain expertise required to define the rubric is concentrated in specialists who are not product managers, as in the wealth advisory case where the rubric required regulatory and financial product knowledge that no PM had.

The dedicated evaluation team model, employed at Anthropic per remarks made by Tom Brown in January 2024, involves a team fully focused on reducing hallucination and factual error rates, with dedicated headcount and infrastructure (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Brown's January 2024 remarks). This model scales to the frontier lab environment but is not accessible to most product teams building LLM-backed applications.

The platform-engineering-led model, documented in Bloomberg's KubeCon series and in the CNCF's emerging platformengineering.org community, assigns retrieval freshness, embedding-index health, PII filtering, and rate-limited tool wrappers to the platform team as shared infrastructure SLOs. The platform team does not own the output quality rubric, but it owns the infrastructure that makes quality measurement possible, including the token-level rate limiting, distributed inference routing, and observability hooks that Bloomberg's Envoy AI Gateway layer provides (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Bloomberg's KubeCon documentation). This model is attractive for organizations with multiple teams building on a shared AI infrastructure, because it distributes the evaluation ownership to the feature teams who understand their specific domain while centralizing the measurement infrastructure.

What the research corpus documents clearly, and what the wealth advisory postmortem illustrates at smaller scale, is the failure mode when none of these models is in place: two teams operating with incompatible measurement frameworks, no organizational structure for adjudicating conflicts between them, and no named boundary that tells each team where its responsibility ends and the other's begins. The absence of the Eval/Ops Split as an explicit organizational construct is itself the failure. The resolution, in every documented case, begins with naming the boundary and assigning clear ownership of each side.

### The Named Sub-Patterns of the Split

The Eval/Ops Split manifests in three recurring sub-patterns that each carry specific toolchain and ownership implications.

**The Silent Regression Pattern.** A model or retrieval component degrades in a way that produces no operational signal. The traces are clean. The error rates are flat. The latency is within the SLO. The evaluation scores, if they are being collected, decline steadily. The Silent Regression Pattern is the most dangerous failure mode in AI systems that monitor only the operational layer, because it is structurally invisible to all operational alerting. The embedding drift incident described at the opening of Chapter 2 is the canonical example from this book's corpus: the operational telemetry showed a healthy system while the retrieval layer was producing semantically wrong results, and the platform team had no instrumentation that could detect the difference.

The appropriate response to the Silent Regression Pattern is not to add more operational metrics. It is to establish a continuous evaluation pipeline with its own alerting layer, tuned to detect directional drift over evaluation windows long enough to absorb the noise in individual judge evaluations. Charity Majors' formulation in her March 2026 post on agent observability captures the mechanism precisely: "If you are trying to do anomaly detection with no a priori knowledge of what to look for, the anomaly has to be fairly large to be detected. But if you do have some knowledge of intent, along with precision tooling, these anomalies can be tracked and validated even when they are exquisitely minute" (Claude SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems report, Q2 2026, citing Majors' March 2026 post). The "knowledge of intent" is the evaluation rubric, the domain-expert-defined standard against which outputs are scored. The "precision tooling" is the calibrated judge. The "anomaly detection" is the trend analysis over a rolling evaluation window. Without all three, the Silent Regression Pattern proceeds until a user or a human reviewer notices. With all three, it is catchable in advance.

**The Over-Instrumentation Pattern.** A team responds to the Eval/Ops Split by routing all evaluation signals into the operational monitoring platform, creating a dashboard where latency SLOs, error rates, hallucination scores, toxicity flags, retrieval relevance scores, and user satisfaction ratings appear side by side without organizational differentiation. The dashboard is complete. It is also unactionable, because the signals have different noise characteristics, different measurement methods, different ownership implications, and different appropriate response playbooks. An SRE arriving at this dashboard during an incident cannot distinguish which signal is operationally urgent from which is evaluatively concerning. A hallucination score decline requires a different response than a latency spike: the former requires routing to the domain-expert coalition for rubric re-evaluation and model assessment, while the latter requires the SRE's standard incident response workflow.

The Over-Instrumentation Pattern is a form of information overload that parallels the cardinality pressure described in earlier chapters of this book. **The Cardinality Cliff**, as the principle-level constraint established in Chapter 3, applies to evaluation metrics just as it applies to custom metrics: instrumenting every conceivable quality signal without defining the ownership and response policy for each signal produces a system with dozens of quality metrics, all of which are simultaneously green, yellow, or red, none of which are connected to a clear escalation path. The operational team watches the evaluation signals because they are visible. The evaluation coalition, if it exists, does not have access to the alerting infrastructure where those signals might fire. The result is a dashboard that records everything and triggers appropriate action on nothing. The solution is not to remove the evaluation signals from view. It is to configure them with the ownership and alert policies appropriate to the evaluation discipline: slow-burn trend alerts routed to the evaluation coalition, with a notification protocol that does not page the SRE on-call for quality signals that require domain-expert interpretation.

**The Service-Drift Blind Spot.** A distinct failure mode documented in recent academic literature (PMC12863567, 2025; longitudinal LLM-judge benchmarks) involves hosted LLM providers making silent updates to the model behind a stable API endpoint. The provider does not announce the update. The model identifier in the span remains unchanged. The operational telemetry shows no disruption. The evaluation scores begin to drift because the model's behavior has changed in ways that are not reflected in any operational signal. The operational toolchain cannot detect this. Only the evaluation toolchain can.

The Slack AI indirect data exfiltration incident from 2024, referenced here in shorthand as documented in the research corpus (Perplexity summary of notable AI-LLM-specific production incidents, Q2 2026), illustrates a related class of provider-side change: a feature update that introduced new attack surfaces that preserved operational health signals while degrading the system's security posture in ways that were evaluatively meaningful. Between August and October 2024, operationally, the system continued to function correctly. From an evaluation perspective, a new attack class had become possible that the operational toolchain could not detect without a specifically configured security evaluation layer. The appropriate monitoring response, treating unannounced behavior shifts detected through evaluation-score drift as an alert condition rather than as normal variation, is an evaluation-layer practice. It requires the evaluation coalition to be watching the evaluation signals independently of the operational dashboard, with the specific sensitivity to detect behavioral change at the model level rather than at the infrastructure level.

### The Toolchain Division

The toolchain implications of the Eval/Ops Split are straightforward to state at the principle level and organizationally difficult to implement consistently. This section describes the principle-level division. The Datadog-specific implementation, including the LLM Observability evaluation surface, the LLM Experiments SDK, the Annotation Queues feature, and the out-of-the-box evaluator templates, is treated in Chapter 7 as part of the broader treatment of the Datadog AI Surface.

The operational toolchain is structured around a time-series database, a trace backend, and an alerting engine. Its primary interface is a dashboard designed for real-time incident response. Its alerting policy is configured for fast detection and fast escalation. Its primary consumers are SREs and platform engineers who need to determine, in minutes, whether the system needs immediate intervention. The operational toolchain excels at recording what happened mechanically: which spans were created, when they opened and closed, what status codes they carried, what resource utilization accompanied them.

The evaluation toolchain is structured around a dataset of sampled interactions, a judge pipeline that scores those interactions against a rubric, and an analytics layer that tracks score distributions over time. Its primary interface is a quality review dashboard designed for trend analysis rather than real-time monitoring. Its alerting policy, where one exists, is configured for slow-burn detection over multi-day windows rather than for fast escalation. Its primary consumers are ML engineers, product managers, and domain experts who need to determine, over days or weeks, whether the model's behavior is drifting in ways that require intervention.

These toolchains can share infrastructure, and the most mature production implementations do share infrastructure at the trace storage layer. A trace backend that stores full interaction payloads, including prompts and responses, enables both operational span analysis and offline evaluation dataset construction from the same underlying store. This is the architectural pattern that Datadog LLM Observability implements: production traces are stored in the LLM Observability backend, and evaluation datasets can be constructed from those stored traces using the LLM Experiments SDK, with human annotation possible through the Annotation Queues feature (Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026). The shared trace storage layer is the bridge between the two disciplines, not a collapse of them. The evaluation pipeline reads from the same store as the operational toolchain. It runs asynchronously, at sampled rates, with its own scoring logic and its own result store. The two disciplines share evidence but not methods, not alerting policies, and not ownership.

Where specialized evaluation tools have their structural advantage is in capabilities that an operational monitoring platform was not designed to provide: prompt version management, structured human annotation queues with multi-reviewer aggregation logic, dataset versioning and ground-truth labeling workflows, CI/CD blocking gates on quality regression, and the ability to run controlled comparisons between two model versions on the same evaluation dataset before a deployment decision. Specialized evaluation tools (Langfuse, LangSmith, Braintrust, Arize Phoenix) provide human annotation queues and CI/CD quality gates not available natively in Datadog as of Q2 2026 (full treatment in Chapter 10). These are evaluation-discipline features that reflect a different product philosophy: that the primary consumer of the toolchain is the team deciding whether to promote a model version to production, not the team managing a 2 a.m. incident.

Datadog LLM Observability's evaluation surface, as of Q2 2026, provides online evaluation with out-of-the-box judge templates for hallucination, failure-to-answer, topic relevancy, toxicity, sentiment, goal completeness, tool selection, tool argument correctness, and prompt injection, alongside the LLM Experiments SDK for offline evaluation against archived datasets (Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026). The Annotation Queues feature, available through the LLM Observability evaluations section, provides a structured workflow for human review: queues can be populated automatically from production traces using Automation Rules that filter by evaluation results, latency thresholds, or application, routing matching traces at up to 5 percent sampling to a named annotation queue (Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026). Human reviewers apply structured labels with multi-reviewer aggregation logic for disagreement resolution, and completed annotations can be exported for dataset construction or CI/CD integration. The maximum queue size of 1,000 records is a documented constraint that reflects the difference between an annotation tool embedded in an operational platform and a purpose-built annotation system designed for large-scale ground-truth labeling. Teams running high-volume evaluation pipelines will reach this limit and need to architect a rotation strategy or supplement with a specialized evaluation tool.

The documented structural gap in Datadog's evaluation surface, as of Q2 2026, is in the pre-deployment blocking gate. Datadog does not provide a native mechanism for blocking a code merge or a model deployment when evaluation scores regress below a configured threshold. That gap belongs to the evaluation discipline as the mechanism for preventing quality regressions from reaching production, and it is where the Eval/Ops Split has the most direct toolchain consequence: the operational platform can record that quality has degraded after a deployment; it cannot prevent the deployment from occurring based on pre-deployment quality evaluation. The specific competitive tooling that addresses this gap is treated in Chapter 10. The resolution at the organizational level is to connect the evaluation toolchain, whether Datadog's LLM Experiments or a specialized tool, to the CI/CD pipeline outside of Datadog.

The assessment here is that the Eval/Ops Split is not an argument for two separate platforms in every case. It is an argument for two separate disciplines, with clear ownership boundaries, clear method boundaries, and clear toolchain boundaries that may or may not map to separate vendor products depending on the team's scale and requirements. A team with low evaluation volume and a platform-centric organizational model may route both disciplines through a single platform and accept the tradeoffs. A team operating at the scale of a Bloomberg AI platform, a Shopify Sidekick, or a Honeycomb Query Assistant, where evaluation is a continuous engineering practice with its own team, its own release gates, and its own alerting policy, requires the separation to be structural and explicit. The important variable is not the number of platforms. It is whether the boundary between the two disciplines has been named, documented, and assigned to specific owners.

---

The wealth advisory team resolved their crisis by naming the distinction and building the coalition: a domain expert from the wealth advisory group became the rubric owner, an ML engineer implemented and calibrated the judge, and the SRE team reconfigured their alerting policy to route evaluation-score alerts to the coalition rather than to the incident response queue. The resolution took four weeks from the postmortem to the first operational evaluation pipeline. It would have taken four hours to design at system inception, if the Split had been in the architecture from the start.

The Eval/Ops Split also clarifies what kind of investment each discipline requires over time. Operational monitoring, once configured correctly, is largely self-sustaining: alerts fire, SREs respond, the infrastructure evolves and the monitors are updated to match. The investment is roughly proportional to the rate of infrastructure change. Evaluation monitoring degrades without active maintenance, because the judge drifts, the model drifts, the user population shifts, and the domain expert who calibrated the rubric may leave the team. The investment is roughly proportional to the rate of change in the model, the provider, and the user population, all of which change more frequently in production AI systems than the infrastructure layer does. Teams that staff and budget the evaluation discipline as if it were operational monitoring, with an initial configuration effort followed by steady-state maintenance, consistently find themselves holding a measurement instrument that has silently become unreliable. The Eval/Ops Split implies not just different toolchains and different ownership. It implies different staffing models, different operational rhythms, and different investment profiles for two disciplines that share the same underlying system.

Two disciplines. One system. The question is not which matters more. It is which team is answering which question, with which tools, on which timeline, and with which rubric as the standard of correctness. When those assignments are clear from the start, quality regressions surface in days and route to the right people. When they are absent, the investigations last weeks and route to the wrong people. The wealth advisory postmortem took five weeks to resolve what a well-designed system would have caught in five days.

### Key Takeaways

The **Eval/Ops Split** is not a tooling argument; it is an epistemological boundary between two questions that require different methods, different evidence types, and different ownership structures. Production observability asks whether the system is healthy, answered by measuring mechanical properties. LLM evaluation asks whether the model is performing well, answered by comparing outputs to a human-defined quality rubric. Conflating these questions in a single dashboard creates a monitoring artifact that is simultaneously complete-looking and diagnostically unreliable: the operational signals are green while a quality regression is accumulating invisibly.

- The **Silent Regression Pattern** is the canonical consequence of the Eval/Ops Split ignored: a model or retrieval component degrades without producing any operational signal, making it structurally invisible to all operational alerting and detectable only through a continuous evaluation pipeline with its own alerting layer.
- The EU AI Act's risk-tier framework requires logging, human oversight mechanisms, and accuracy claims for systems in high-risk sectors; operational observability alone does not satisfy these requirements, because the required evidence is in the evaluation layer.
- SLOs for non-deterministic AI systems require judge-human calibration before they can be trusted: Phillip Carter's account of calibrating the Honeycomb Query Assistant SLO (three iterations to reach greater than 90 percent judge-human agreement) demonstrates that the calibration work is a prerequisite for the SLO's validity, not a post-deployment refinement.
- The boundary between the two disciplines is where they share signals: latency, error rate, and structural correctness appear in both operational monitoring and evaluation monitoring, but they mean different things in each context and route to different teams with different response playbooks.
- Evaluation monitoring requires more maintenance investment than operational monitoring because it degrades with judge drift, model drift, and user population shift, all of which occur faster in AI systems than the infrastructure changes that require operational monitoring updates.
- Datadog's evaluation surface at Q2 2026 covers out-of-the-box judge templates and the LLM Experiments SDK; it does not cover pre-deployment blocking gates, fully customizable rubrics with human annotation at scale, or ground-truth comparison datasets for safety-critical applications.
