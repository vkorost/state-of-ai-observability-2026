# Chapter 3: Cardinality, Sampling, and the Cost of Knowing

*In this chapter, the analysis turns to the cost mechanics that make AI observability structurally more expensive than traditional APM. Cardinality, the number of unique values a tag dimension can take, compounds superlinearly in AI workloads because the data these systems produce is semantically rich and inherently unbounded. The chapter traces how AI-assisted code generation accelerates the problem, lays out the three sampling strategies and the failure mode each accepts, and argues that the decision of what not to observe is a first-class engineering discipline whose absence shows up on a billing page weeks after a feature ships.*

The engineering team at a mid-market SaaS company noticed something unusual in October 2024, about three weeks after they shipped their first production AI feature: a conversational assistant that helped customers navigate complex configuration workflows. The feature launched smoothly. Response quality was acceptable. Latency was within tolerance. User satisfaction scores moved modestly upward.

The bill did not move modestly.

In the first month after the assistant went live, the team's Datadog invoice increased by approximately 180 percent. The growth in infrastructure costs, in LLM API fees, in cloud egress: none of those explained it. The spike was concentrated in custom metrics and indexed spans. When the team's platform engineer began investigating, she found a pattern that would repeat across many organizations following a similar path: the AI feature had been instrumented by a small, fast-moving product squad using AI-assisted code generation tools, and that instrumentation had been generated with exactly the level of careful cardinality review the rest of the codebase received under deadline pressure. Which is to say, none.<sup>[1,2]</sup>

The assistant logged a metric for every LLM call. The metric carried tags for model name, environment, response latency bucket, token count bucket, and, fatally, the first 32 characters of the user's query. That last tag turned a manageable metric into an unbounded time series. Every unique query prefix became a unique tag value. Every unique combination of that tag with the other five tags became a distinct, billable custom metric time series. Within three weeks of launch, a single metric name was generating more than 400,000 unique time series. The observability cost of the feature had exceeded the combined cost of the LLM API calls the feature was making and the compute that served it.<sup>[1,2]</sup>

The fix was straightforward once identified: remove the query-prefix tag, replace it with a hashed session identifier joinable against a separate log store. Cardinality collapsed. The bill returned to a manageable level. The team had spent three weeks solving a problem that a five-minute cardinality review at the time of instrumentation would have prevented.

## The Superlinear Machine

What makes this team's story relevant is not the bill. Bill shock is an old story in the observability industry. What is new, and what the AI era has made structurally more dangerous, is the combination of forces that made the mistake so easy to make and so expensive once made. The AI-assisted coding tools that generated the instrumentation were doing exactly what they were designed to do: producing comprehensive, verbose, well-labeled telemetry. The platform billed for every distinct combination of metric name and tag value, as it was documented to do. No individual decision was wrong. The system produced a predictably bad outcome from a set of locally reasonable decisions made without visibility into their aggregate cost.

**Cardinality**, in the observability context, refers to the number of unique values a dimension can take. A metric tagged with environment is low-cardinality: most production systems have two to five environments. A metric tagged with http.status_code is also low-cardinality: perhaps twenty status codes are in active use across a typical web service. A metric tagged with user_id is high-cardinality: in any system with meaningful adoption, the number of unique users is effectively unbounded.

The billing consequence emerges from how multi-dimensional time series work. A metric with three tags, where the first tag has 5 values, the second has 20 values, and the third has 100 values, can produce up to 10,000 unique time series. In practice the combination space is never fully saturated, but for high-cardinality tag values the multiplication still drives the total time series count far above what any individual tag count would suggest. Datadog counts custom metrics as the number of unique combinations of metric name, host tag, and key-value tag pairs observed over a trailing hour (per Datadog custom metrics billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026). Overages beyond the included plan allocation are charged at approximately $0.05 per time series per month.<sup>[3]</sup>

A worked example makes the multiplication concrete. Suppose a team instruments a single LLM-call metric, `llm.request.duration`, with four tags:

| Tag | Example values | Cardinality |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `env` | prod, staging | 2 |
| `model` | gpt-4o, claude-sonnet | 2 |
| `status` | 200, 429, 500 | 3 |
| `query_prefix_32` | (first 32 chars of user input) | unbounded |

With the first three tags alone, the metric produces at most 12 time series (2 times 2 times 3). Safe. Now add the query prefix. If 50,000 unique users send queries in a month, the query prefix tag may produce 30,000 or more distinct values. The total time series count jumps to 12 times 30,000: 360,000. At $0.05 per series per month, that single metric costs approximately $18,000 per month in overages, before any other custom metric is counted. Remove the query prefix tag, and the metric costs effectively nothing above the plan inclusion. That superlinearity is the defining feature of the cardinality problem. One tag dimension with tens of thousands of unique values does not cost modestly more. It costs orders of magnitude more.

**The Cardinality Cliff.** The point at which one new high-entropy tag dimension causes observability storage cost to compound superlinearly, a threshold that AI workloads reach faster than traditional APM because prompt and response text naturally inhabits tag positions that APM was designed to keep low-cardinality. The cliff is not approached gradually. It appears at the moment a high-cardinality value enters a tag position, and the cost increase is immediate and ongoing for as long as the metric exists in indexed form.

The Cardinality Cliff has existed in observability platforms since custom metrics became a standard feature. What changes in the AI era is not the mathematics of the cliff but the speed and regularity with which the current development workflow drives teams toward it.

## The Volume Problem in the Log Layer

Cardinality in the metrics layer has a sibling in the log layer, though the mechanism is different. Logs do not have cardinality in the same sense as metrics: log events are records, not time series, and they are billed by volume (ingestion in gigabytes) and by indexing count (events retained in searchable storage), not by the number of unique field value combinations. But the log layer faces its own version of the same pressure. When an AI system emits prompt and response text as log fields, the log volume scales with the verbosity of the model and the depth of the conversation. A multi-turn conversation with a 50,000-token context window can produce a single log event several hundred kilobytes in size. A system generating ten thousand such conversations per day produces several gigabytes of log data from the AI layer alone. At standard log indexing rates (per Datadog log management pricing documentation, accessed Q2 2026), this volume becomes a significant line item before any operational logs, error logs, or access logs are counted.<sup>[3,4]</sup>

The AI system produces log events that are individually much larger than traditional log events, because the content being logged, conversational AI context, is semantically dense and not reducible to structured fields without losing diagnostic value. Teams that apply the same log retention and indexing policies to AI payload logs as to their traditional application logs encounter a proportional scaling of log costs that the traditional policy was not designed to absorb. The disciplined response is a separate log-routing policy for AI payload data: not indexed in the primary log backend at full retention, but archived to object storage and accessible via rehydration when a specific investigation requires it. This routing decision is a log-layer analog of the sampling decision in the trace layer. The team accepts reduced day-to-day query convenience in exchange for a cost structure that scales with the value of the data rather than with its raw volume.

## How AI-Assisted Development Accelerates the Problem

For most of the history of production software development, telemetry was expensive to write. Adding a metric required understanding the billing implications of the tags chosen. Adding a log statement meant considering whether the log would be indexed, at what retention level, and at what volume. The effort of getting these decisions right created a natural forcing function: developers who wrote instrumentation manually were compelled to think, at least briefly, about what they were measuring and why.

AI-assisted code generation tools changed this cost structure. Cursor, GitHub Copilot, Claude Code, Cline, Codex, and similar tools generate instrumentation code as part of standard boilerplate output. A developer who asks an AI coding assistant to add observability to a new LLM service receives, in seconds, a complete instrumentation configuration: verbose logging, comprehensive trace captures, detailed error handlers, and metric tags capturing whatever the developer considered relevant at the time of composing the prompt.<sup>[1]</sup>

The phenomenon has a straightforward mechanism. AI coding assistants lower the marginal cost of writing code to near zero, causing a proliferation of services, endpoints, and telemetry configurations that is not matched by a corresponding investment in observability discipline. The result is superlinear growth in cardinality and cost. This is not a failure of the AI tools. It is the predictable consequence of removing friction from a process that previously relied on that friction as a forcing function for deliberation.

Before AI coding assistants, the effort of writing instrumentation manually created a natural pause. A developer composing metric tags typed each key and value, which created a moment of consideration: is this the right tag? Does this value have bounded cardinality? That pause was informal, inconsistent, and often skipped under deadline pressure, but it existed. When the assistant generates the full instrumentation block, the pause disappears. The developer reviews output rather than composing it, and review is cognitively different from composition. Reviewers accept plausible-looking output that they would not have composed themselves. A tag like query_prefix_32chars looks reasonable on a code review; its cardinality implications are not visible from the code itself.

Automated code deployments have driven custom metric series registrations up by a factor of 20 in observed cases, quickly triggering platform rate limits and billing overages.<sup>[1]</sup> The same pattern holds for log volume: sudden spikes typically reflect accidental looping behaviors or unoptimized debug configurations left in AI-generated microservices rather than planned feature releases. The instrumentation arrives with the feature, mirrors the feature's reach, and persists in the billing register long after the team has moved to the next sprint.

The organizational dimension compounds the technical one. AI-assisted development has reduced the size of the team needed to ship a feature. A product squad of two engineers and an AI coding assistant can produce and deploy a service in weeks that would previously have required a larger team over several months. The smaller team has less institutional knowledge of the platform's billing model, less interaction with the SRE or platform team that manages observability spend, and less exposure to the prior incidents that established the organization's cardinality discipline. When each of those smaller, faster squads adds its own instrumentation without coordination across a shared metric namespace, the aggregate cardinality grows without any single squad being aware of it.<sup>[1]</sup>

Platform engineering teams that have absorbed this lesson are addressing it through **telemetry budgets**: per-team allocations of custom metric series and indexed span volume enforced at the pipeline level, with automated alerts when any team approaches their allocation. A tag-governance configuration fragment, applied at the collector or pipeline layer, illustrates the structural enforcement:

```yaml
# Tag governance: allow/deny rules at the OTel Collector processor level
processors:
  attributes/cardinality_guard:
    actions:
      # Strip high-entropy tags before they reach the metric backend
      - key: query_prefix
        action: delete
      - key: user_id
        action: delete
      - key: raw_prompt
        action: delete
      # Permit bounded, governed dimensions
      - key: env
        action: upsert
      - key: model_name
        action: upsert
      - key: service
        action: upsert
```

This approach treats cardinality governance as a resource management problem analogous to CPU or memory quotas. The enforcement is structural rather than advisory, and it creates a feedback loop that the natural friction of hand-written instrumentation used to provide.

The interaction between AI-assisted code generation and the AI application surface (the full set of new observable components generative AI systems introduce, established in Chapter 2) means that the cardinality exposure is not limited to instrumentation of the LLM call layer. Each layer of the AI application stack, the LLM call, the embedding pipeline, the retrieval layer, the agent orchestration loop, and the multi-model chain, can independently generate high-cardinality telemetry if instrumented without discipline. A team that applies careful tag governance to its LLM metrics but neglects the retrieval layer, tagging retrieval spans with document chunk identifiers or raw query text, has addressed one vector of cardinality risk while leaving others fully exposed.

## Why AI Workloads Hit the Cliff Faster

The acceleration through AI-assisted code generation explains one pathway to the Cardinality Cliff: instrumentation generated without cardinality discipline. But AI workloads amplify cardinality pressure through mechanisms that are not about instrumentation practice at all. They are structural properties of the data that AI systems produce.

Traditional APM metadata has a property that observability platform architects relied on implicitly: it is bounded. An HTTP method has nine possible values. An HTTP status code has perhaps thirty in active use. A database table name has hundreds at most in any given service. The telemetry taxonomy was built on the assumption that tag values come from controlled vocabularies.

Prompt and response text does not come from a controlled vocabulary. The text that a user sends to an LLM assistant is unconstrained natural language. If either prompt or response is used as a tag value, or if any significant substring of either is used as a tag value, the cardinality is effectively infinite. The number of unique 32-character prefixes of user queries for any real production assistant is bounded only by the number of users and the diversity of their questions, both of which scale with adoption in a healthy product.

This structural property means that the cardinality danger for AI workloads is not limited to careless instrumentation. It also emerges from well-intentioned instrumentation that captures semantically useful information in structurally dangerous positions. The team whose story opened this chapter was not careless. They wanted to see which types of queries were generating high token costs. That is a legitimate diagnostic goal. The mechanism they chose, a metric tag carrying query text, was incompatible with the cardinality constraints of the platform regardless of how reasonable it seemed from the developer's perspective.

The pattern repeats in other forms. A model version identifier used as a metric tag is safe when a team deploys model versions monthly, but becomes a cardinality problem when continuous delivery introduces dozens of fine-tuned checkpoints per week. A user identifier in a span attribute is tolerable when the span is sampled at low rates, but becomes a cardinality crisis when the span is indexed at full fidelity and the user base grows by an order of magnitude. A retrieval relevance score bucketed into ten ranges is manageable; the same score stored as a floating-point metric dimension with four decimal places of precision is not.

Agentic systems extend the problem further. A multi-step agent session generates between 40 and 75 spans per user interaction, compared to two or three spans for a typical web service request.<sup>[1]</sup> Each of those spans carries its own tag set. If those tags include session-scoped or request-scoped identifiers, the cardinality multiplication affects not one metric but dozens of metrics simultaneously. An agent handling 10,000 user sessions per day, with each session producing 50 spans and each span tagged with a session identifier, generates 500,000 span records per day with a session-scoped dimension. At any retention window beyond a single day, the custom metric cardinality and indexed span volumes compound rapidly.

The cost consequence is concrete. Independent analyses of Datadog billing for AI workloads report that adding generative AI monitoring to existing production stacks increases observability bills by 40 to 200 percent in documented cases, and that in some configurations the observability cost for an LLM-backed system exceeds the cost of the LLM API calls being observed.<sup>[2,5]</sup> A customer support bot handling 50,000 user messages per day generates approximately 200,000 LLM invocations and 1,000,000 spans per day, alongside several million metric data points and hundreds of megabytes of logs: ten to fifty times the telemetry volume of an equivalent traditional service.<sup>[5]</sup> At Datadog's documented APM pricing of $1.70 per million indexed spans beyond the included allocation (per Datadog APM billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026), this accumulates to a significant ongoing cost that teams frequently do not model before committing to full-fidelity instrumentation.<sup>[4]</sup>

Cardinality risk for AI workloads is not a matter of discipline alone. Even a disciplined, careful team will find that the semantic richness of AI telemetry creates recurring pressure toward high-cardinality representations that are diagnostic but structurally expensive. Managing that pressure is an ongoing engineering activity, not a one-time configuration choice.

## Cross-Pillar Cost Compounding

Cardinality pressure in AI workloads does not operate in isolation. It interacts with the other two pillars of the observability model in ways that compound the financial exposure beyond what any single-pillar analysis would suggest. Understanding this cross-pillar interaction is necessary for building a cost model that reflects what teams actually encounter when they instrument an AI system at production scale.

Consider what happens when a production RAG-backed assistant is instrumented without cross-pillar coordination. At the metrics layer, token count metrics accumulate cardinality as described above: model-scoped, environment-scoped, and user-tier-scoped custom metrics multiply across the combination space. At the logs layer, the same system emits full prompt and response payloads as log events, because the development team correctly identified that the log store is the appropriate place for unstructured text. At the trace layer, every LLM call, every retrieval step, every embedding operation, and every tool invocation generates a span, and the agent session that contains them generates a root span with dozens to hundreds of children.

Each pillar is instrumented with individual discipline. The metrics are tagged at appropriate granularity. The logs are emitted to a structured log store. The spans are propagated with correct context. But the three pillars are not independent. The log volume for a system generating 1,000,000 LLM spans per day, each with a prompt and response payload averaging 5 kilobytes, is 5 gigabytes per day of log data from the AI layer alone, before any infrastructure, application, or access logs are counted. At Datadog's documented log pricing of $0.10 per gigabyte for ingestion plus $1.70 to $2.55 per million events for indexed retention (per Datadog log management pricing and APM billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026), this log volume produces a persistent daily cost that the team did not model when they decided that logging prompts and responses was the correct instrumentation choice.<sup>[1,4]</sup>

The same system's span layer, at 1,000,000 spans per day with full indexing at thirty-day retention, accumulates thirty million indexed spans in steady state. The metrics layer, at four to six custom metric series per LLM call with model, environment, and service dimensions, adds several million active custom metric data points per day. The three cost components, log ingestion, indexed span storage, and custom metric series counts, each individually justified by legitimate instrumentation decisions, multiply together into a total daily cost that can reach several hundred dollars per day for a moderately sized AI application, and several thousand dollars per day for a high-volume one.

Independent cost analyses document a test case that illustrates this compounding precisely. The OpenObserve benchmark of the Datadog APM pricing model used a sixteen-microservice OpenTelemetry demo application augmented with a single LLM recommendation service making LLM API calls. The total Datadog bill for this demo workload reached approximately $174 per day. Of that total, approximately $120 per day was attributed to LLM Observability activation triggered by the presence of OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic attributes on the LLM spans. The remaining $54 per day covered APM host fees, indexed span storage for the non-LLM services, and infrastructure monitoring. The LLM service generated only 232 LLM spans in the test period. The implication is that the activation threshold for LLM Observability premium billing, estimated at approximately $120 per day in floor cost once GenAI-attributed spans flow into the platform, is not volume-dependent at lower span counts (per OpenObserve traces and APM benchmark, December 2025; per Perplexity independent cost analyses, May 2026).<sup>[2,6]</sup>

This billing behavior, where the presence of GenAI-attributed telemetry can trigger a category change in billing tier independent of the volume of that telemetry, is a qualitatively different cost dynamic from the per-unit pricing that teams model when they plan their observability spend. Per-unit pricing allows teams to model cost as a linear function of instrumentation decisions: add N spans, pay for N spans. Tier-activation pricing means that crossing a threshold triggers a step-change in cost that is independent of volume below the threshold. Teams that instrument their first LLM-backed feature and emit its spans to their existing Datadog organization without modeling this activation behavior discover the step-change on their first post-launch invoice, not during the instrumentation planning phase.<sup>[2,6]</sup>

The cross-pillar awareness this requires is practical: before adding AI telemetry to an existing observability deployment, model the cost implications across all three pillars simultaneously, including any tier-activation effects, and design the instrumentation to stay within the planned cost envelope from the first day of production traffic. Retrofitting cardinality discipline, sampling policy, and log routing after the first bill arrives costs more in engineering time and business disruption than front-loading those decisions into the instrumentation design.

## Sampling as Cost Engineering

The natural response to cardinality and volume pressure is sampling: not forwarding every event to the telemetry backend, but forwarding a representative subset. Sampling is not a concession to cost constraints. It is a legitimate engineering decision about what the team needs to know and at what resolution. The question is not whether to sample but which sampling strategy best matches the team's actual diagnostic priorities, because each strategy accepts a different failure mode.

The three-way tension is between coverage completeness, cost control, and diagnostic fidelity. Coverage completeness is the ability to observe every request and every span. Cost control is the ability to stay within a financial budget without sacrificing the operational awareness that enables incident response. Diagnostic fidelity is the ability to reconstruct the exact evidence needed to investigate a specific failure: the precise prompt, the full span tree, the sequence of tool calls, the model's response at the moment the problem occurred. Every sampling strategy makes an explicit or implicit choice about which of the three to sacrifice, and that choice must be made deliberately rather than by default.

**Head-based sampling** decides whether to retain a trace at the moment the root span is created, before the system knows how the trace will unfold. The sampling decision is made with zero information about what the trace will contain. A team running ten percent head-based sampling observes ten percent of all user interactions, selected uniformly at random. Coverage is proportional, cost is predictable, and diagnostic fidelity for sampled traces is complete. For unsampled traces, no evidence exists. When an error occurs, there is a 90 percent chance it occurred in a trace that was discarded at ingestion.

For traditional APM workloads with high error rates and consistent trace shapes, head-based sampling is tolerable because sampled errors are statistically representative of unsampled errors. For AI workloads, this assumption breaks down. Error rates are low, failure modes are rare and idiosyncratic, and the most expensive and interesting events are the outliers. A hallucination that causes a user-visible problem may occur in one in ten thousand requests. At ten percent head-based sampling, the expected number of retained instances of that hallucination before the team identifies the pattern is very low. Weeks of exposure may pass before the failure appears in the retained corpus with enough instances to form a detectable signal.

**Tail-based sampling** defers the retention decision until the trace is complete. The collector or sampling proxy observes the full trace, including total latency, error status, span count, and token cost, before deciding whether to retain it. This allows the sampling policy to be governed by trace-level properties: retain 100 percent of error traces, retain 100 percent of traces exceeding a latency threshold, retain 100 percent of traces exceeding a cost threshold, and apply a conservative sample rate of five to ten percent to healthy, fast, cheap traces.<sup>[1]</sup> The result is a retained corpus disproportionately rich in interesting events and economical in volume.

The failure mode for tail-based sampling is infrastructure complexity and the buffering requirement. The collector must hold the complete trace in memory until the root span closes. For short-lived traces, this is manageable. For agentic workflows running for minutes or hours, buffering the full trace before making a retention decision is not practically feasible at scale: a multi-step agent session running for four minutes and producing three hundred spans cannot be held in a collector's memory for four minutes without significant resource overhead, especially when the collector handles many concurrent sessions. The implementation requires a stateful collector with correct context propagation so all spans for a given trace arrive at the same collector instance, and a retention policy specific to the team's actual priorities.

**Exemplar-based sampling** maintains a pool of retained traces that is representative of the full distribution of observed behaviors. The pool is updated continuously: as new traces arrive, the sampling policy selects traces that are most structurally different from those already retained. The pool covers slow traces, fast traces, expensive traces, cheap traces, error traces, successful traces, and unusual traces falling outside the expected performance envelope. The goal is coverage of the behavioral space rather than proportional volume sampling.

The practical challenge for exemplar sampling in AI workloads is defining the distance metric. A trace from an agentic workflow differs from another trace along many dimensions: total duration, span count, token cost, tool invocation pattern, error presence, model used, retrieval relevance distribution. Selecting traces that are maximally different requires a distance function across this multi-dimensional space, and that function requires tuning for the specific workload and its characteristic failure modes. No general-purpose exemplar sampling policy covers the full diversity of AI workload trace shapes without significant configuration investment.

The layered sampling architecture documented in the 2026 reference architecture for AI-heavy Datadog deployments addresses the tension by combining strategies. Tail-based sampling is applied at the edge collector: retain 100 percent of errors, latency exceptions, and guardrail violations; apply a five to ten percent random sample to healthy interactions. High-volume verbose logs are routed to inexpensive self-hosted storage such as ClickHouse, with filtered high-value records forwarded to Datadog for indexed retention. Hot indexed storage in Datadog is limited to a 14-to-15-day window, with older records archived to object storage and available through rehydration when needed.<sup>[1]</sup> This layered approach accepts the operational complexity of multiple data paths in exchange for cost control that any single-strategy approach cannot achieve.

## What Not to Observe

The hardest discipline in observability is not deciding what to instrument. It is deciding what not to instrument. The observability instinct is to capture everything and filter later. This instinct made sense when telemetry generation was expensive relative to storage. In the AI era, both dimensions have shifted. Telemetry generation is cheap (AI coding assistants produce it at near-zero marginal cost). Indexed storage is expensive (span-based billing charges per retained record). The economics have inverted.

The inversion means that generating telemetry without intention has immediate and permanent cost consequences. The team that captures a high-cardinality tag dimension at instrumentation time cannot easily reduce its cardinality without deploying a code change. The metric continues to accumulate unique time series for as long as the instrumented code runs. The decision made at instrumentation time is, in practice, a commitment that persists until someone identifies and fixes it, which in a fast-moving codebase may not happen for months.

Deciding what not to observe requires answering a question that most teams avoid: what evidence would the team need to investigate the specific failure modes this system is architecturally likely to encounter? Not every conceivable failure. The specific, named categories of failure that the system's architecture and usage patterns make probable. For a RAG-backed assistant, the probable failures are retrieval quality degradation, embedding drift, context window exhaustion, and LLM response quality regression. Each failure requires specific telemetry primitives: retrieval relevance score distributions, embedding model version tracking, input token distribution monitoring, and a quality signal on model output. None of these requires session-level query text in a metric tag. The diagnostic evidence is in the span attributes and structured log fields, not in the cardinality-multiplying positions of the metric time series.

Framing instrumentation design around anticipated failure modes produces telemetry configurations that are both cheaper and more useful. The team that knows it needs to detect retrieval degradation instruments the retrieval layer specifically: it tracks similarity score distributions per index and per embedding model version, monitors context window utilization trends, and alerts on significant changes in the proportion of low-relevance retrievals. The team that decides to observe everything and hopes to find the signal later accumulates cardinality debt across every dimension it touched, and the signal it actually needs is often buried in a billing-driven purge of data it could not afford to keep.

The tension between completeness and cost is the central engineering judgment this chapter names. It does not resolve in favor of either extreme. A team that captures no prompt or response data has no evidence for quality regressions. A team that captures all prompt and response data at full fidelity in a metric tag has a bill that grows without bound. The principled middle position is to capture what the team can cite a specific diagnostic use case for, in the storage tier that matches the time-value of that diagnostic use case, at the sampling rate that gives adequate coverage of the failure mode in question.

The practical technique for enforcing this discipline is the telemetry design review: a structured exercise conducted before instrumentation is written, not after the bill arrives. The review asks five questions for each proposed telemetry signal:

First, which specific failure mode does this signal enable the team to detect? If the answer is "all failures" or "we're not sure yet," the signal is not ready for production.

Second, what is the maximum cardinality of the dimensions attached to this signal? If any dimension is unbounded, the signal does not belong in the metric tier.

Third, what is the time-value decay of this signal: how long after an event will the team realistically query the telemetry it produced? If the answer is less than fifteen days for success cases, the retention policy should reflect that.

Fourth, what sampling rate provides adequate statistical coverage for the detection purpose while staying within the team's cost budget? The answer should be computed, not assumed.

Fifth, is there a lower-cost storage tier that provides the same diagnostic value? Many signals that teams put in indexed metrics belong in span attributes or log fields, where they are accessible for investigation but do not contribute to the billable cardinality count.

Teams that run this review before instrumentation is written report that it consistently eliminates 30 to 70 percent of the custom metric series they would have otherwise deployed, with no loss of diagnostic capability for the failure modes they actually monitor (per Datadog custom metrics governance documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[3]</sup>

## The Token Cost Double-Count

One cardinality trap specific to AI workloads deserves isolated treatment: the interaction between LLM token metrics and custom metric billing. Token counts are among the most operationally useful metrics for AI systems. They drive per-call cost, correlate with latency, indicate whether the system is operating within its context window budget, and provide the primary signal for detecting runaway consumption before it reaches billing-cycle close. The natural instrumentation approach records token counts as custom metrics, tagged by model, environment, and service.

The danger is in the dimensions that teams layer onto these metrics under the reasonable assumption that more granular attribution is more useful. A token count metric tagged with model name and environment has bounded cardinality. The same metric tagged with model name, environment, user tier, feature flag variant, and A/B test bucket has cardinality that multiplies with each additional dimension. At the scale and velocity that AI-assisted development enables, the number of active feature flag variants and A/B test buckets grows continuously, and each new dimension in the metric tag set compounds the cardinality of every dimension already present.

Independent cost analyses document that token-based custom metrics, when derived from spans and tagged with granular dimensions, can produce additional custom metric charges rivaling the span storage costs themselves.<sup>[2]</sup> This means that a team instrumenting its AI system thoroughly, with token counts, latency histograms, and cost-per-call metrics at the model-environment-user-segment level, can generate a metric footprint exceeding the team's entire pre-AI total custom metric usage before the end of the first month of operation.

LLM Observability in Datadog uses APM's span-based billing as its foundation, which means token metrics derived from span attributes inherit the same cardinality and indexing economics as any other custom metric (per Datadog APM billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026; per AI Superior consulting analysis, April 2026).<sup>[2,7]</sup> This creates a specific instrumentation anti-pattern: using span-derived token data to generate custom metrics at a granularity that the span itself does not need. The token count per span is a span attribute, stored at the span storage tier. Deriving from it a custom metric tagged with session-level or user-level dimensions promotes that data into the custom metric tier, where it is billed at a different and often higher rate than the original span.

The principle-level response is the same as the response to the general cardinality problem. Token metrics should be tagged at the coarsest granularity that still permits the operational question to be answered. Model name and environment are almost always necessary. User tier may be necessary for chargeback allocation. User identifier, session identifier, and individual request identifier are almost never appropriate on a metric, even when they are necessary in a span attribute or a log field. The distinction between metric tags and span attributes is, in practice, a distinction between what must be indexed at the metric tier and what can be stored less expensively in the span or log tier.

## The Retention Gradient

The economic analysis of what to observe, and at what resolution, extends naturally into retention. Not all telemetry has the same time-value decay. An error trace from a production failure that occurred four hours ago is maximally valuable: the on-call engineer is actively investigating, the context is fresh, and the trace is the primary evidence for root cause analysis. The same error trace from eight months ago is of archival interest only.

Traditional APM handled this with a simple retention window: keep everything for fifteen or thirty days, discard it afterward. The economics of that policy were tolerable because traditional APM data volumes were low enough that thirty-day full retention was not prohibitively expensive for most organizations. AI workloads change the volume assumption fundamentally. A system generating one million spans per day at a thirty-day retention policy requires thirty million indexed spans in storage at steady state. At Datadog's documented APM pricing (per Datadog APM billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026), this produces an indexed span cost that is independent of whether those spans are ever queried and insensitive to the fact that 99 percent of them will never be accessed after the first 48 hours.<sup>[4]</sup>

A retention gradient policy responds to this by differentiating retention by signal type and age. High-value records, including error traces, latency outliers, and traces associated with flagged quality signals such as detected prompt injection or low hallucination scores, are retained at full fidelity for the full configured window or longer, because their diagnostic value persists across the investigation lifecycle. Standard records, including normal-behavior traces from healthy sessions, are retained for a shorter window. Seven to fifteen days is a common recommendation from independent cost analyses, reflecting the empirical observation that healthy traces are rarely queried beyond a week.<sup>[2]</sup> Records that must be preserved for compliance reasons are moved to low-cost object storage rather than remaining in the indexed backend, where they would continue to incur per-record indexing costs. Full-text prompt and response payloads, when retained at all, belong in this archive tier with a join key connecting them to the parent trace.

The retention gradient is distinct from sampling. Sampling decides at ingestion time which records to forward. The retention gradient decides at storage time how long to keep records that were already ingested. The two policies interact: a team that applies aggressive tail-based sampling at ingestion and a retention gradient at storage achieves cost control through complementary mechanisms, each targeting a different stage of the telemetry pipeline. Sampling reduces volume before storage; the gradient reduces cost for records correctly retained at ingestion but whose diagnostic value has subsequently decayed.

## The Signal the Team Is Paying to Miss

The most uncomfortable implication of the sampling tension is that every sampling strategy produces a set of events the team will never see: the signal that was generated, transmitted, and then discarded before reaching the indexed backend. For traditional APM workloads, discarded signal was mostly redundant: a tenth instance of the same error pattern in the same service. Discarding those records cost little in diagnostic value.

For AI workloads, the discarded signal can be the most valuable record in the set. A hallucination causing a user-visible harm may occur once in a million requests. At any economically rational sample rate, the trace containing that hallucination is likely to be among the discarded majority. A prompt injection attack that succeeded on the first attempt leaves no record in a system sampling only five percent of healthy-looking traces, because the attack produced a structurally normal trace with normal latency and no error code.

This is not an argument against sampling. It is an argument for what the sampling policy must protect. For AI workloads, the preserved fraction must include not only operational failures and latency outliers but also semantic anomalies: traces where model behavior was unusual in ways that cannot be detected from operational signals alone. Detecting semantic anomalies at sampling time requires either full-trace inspection (expensive and complex at scale) or a fast signal that can be computed cheaply and used to override the default sampling decision. Guardrail evaluations, out-of-the-box prompt injection detectors, and lightweight hallucination classifiers serve this function: they run quickly against each trace at ingestion time and produce a retention flag that preserves the trace regardless of its operational characteristics.

The cost of running evaluations at ingestion time is nontrivial. Running a full LLM-as-a-Judge quality evaluation against every trace at ingestion is often more expensive than the original instrumentation. But running a fast classifier against all traces, and reserving the full evaluation for traces flagged by the classifier, provides a middle path: the traces the team genuinely needed to see have a materially higher probability of being retained, without requiring that every trace be evaluated at full evaluation cost. This is the pattern that makes sampling compatible with quality signal coverage: not a flat sample rate, but a policy that samples the ordinary and retains the anomalous.

Independent practitioners report that evaluation pipelines run synchronously against all production traffic can be several times more expensive than the base tracing infrastructure.<sup>[5]</sup> The economic implication is that the decision to run quality evaluations is not solely an observability architecture decision. It is also a cost commitment that must be modeled alongside the instrumentation, sampling, and retention decisions. A team that designs a careful cardinality-disciplined metric taxonomy, applies tail-based sampling at ingestion, and then adds synchronous LLM-as-a-Judge evaluations on all retained traces has potentially shifted its primary cost driver from span storage to evaluation compute. The total cost model must include all three layers: instrumentation cost, storage cost, and evaluation cost. Optimizing one layer without modeling the others produces a cost-shifted outcome rather than a cost-reduced one.

The sampling strategy choice determines whether a runaway cost event shows up as a catastrophic signal or a modest statistical elevation: the $47,000 multi-agent cost loop documented in Chapter 6 illustrates that distinction, because the platform recorded the loop in near-real-time while head-based sampling attenuated the amplitude enough to prevent alerting.<sup>[8]</sup>

## The Four-Layer Cost Model

The observation that AI observability cost is multi-dimensional, with independent drivers at the instrumentation, storage, and evaluation layers, is the cross-pillar awareness this chapter has been building toward. The Cardinality Cliff operates at the instrumentation and metric-storage layer. The sampling tension operates at the ingestion layer. The retention gradient operates at the long-term storage layer. And the evaluation cost operates at the quality-signal layer that sits above the trace. A principled cost engineering practice treats all four layers as a single system, with trade-offs flowing across them, rather than optimizing each layer in isolation and discovering the aggregate cost at invoice time.

The question of what not to observe, then, has a more precise formulation than "reduce cardinality and sample more aggressively." It is: design the sampling and instrumentation policies so that the signal with the highest diagnostic value for the failure modes the team expects to encounter is the signal least likely to be discarded. Everything else is cost engineering in service of that constraint.

A team that understands this distinction will invest less in instruments that capture everything and more in instruments that capture the right things. It will not put session-level query text in a metric tag, because the diagnostic value does not justify the cardinality cost. It will capture prompt injection evaluations on a sampled fraction of traffic, because the diagnostic value of detecting an injection event is high and the cost of missing one is also high. It will retain error traces at full fidelity and apply aggressive sampling to healthy traces, because the time-value of a healthy trace decays within hours while the time-value of an error trace can persist for days.

The **Three-Pillar Strain**, established in Chapter 1 as the structural mismatch between the metrics-logs-traces model and the properties of AI workloads, expresses itself most concretely at the cardinality dimension. Metrics accumulate superlinear cardinality from AI data. Logs grow to payload sizes that make indexing prohibitively expensive. Traces must represent execution topologies that were not designed into the original span model. The response to each of these pressures is not to instrument less, but to instrument differently: with discipline, with deliberate retention policies, and with a clear accounting of what the team is choosing not to know and why.

Cost and signal are not in opposition. They are in tension, and the discipline of cardinality engineering is the practice of managing that tension with precision rather than luck. The Cardinality Cliff does not announce itself. It appears on a Tuesday, on a billing page, three weeks after a feature shipped.

That is the gap cardinality discipline exists to close.

## Key Points

- Prompt text, completion text, and session IDs must never occupy metric tag dimensions; route them to span attributes or log tiers.
- Tail-based sampling with error and cost predicates is required for AI workloads; head-based sampling underrepresents the runaway loops and quality outliers that matter most.
- AI-assisted code generation lowers the friction that once forced cardinality review; teams must reimpose that review deliberately before instrumentation reaches production.
- A single AI feature can simultaneously expand custom metric cardinality, log payload size, and indexed span count; per-pillar cost governance misses the interaction effects.
- Deciding what not to observe is a first-class decision: a pre-instrumentation design review routinely eliminates 30 to 70 percent of planned metric series.
