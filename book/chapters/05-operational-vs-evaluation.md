# Chapter 5: Operational Metrics vs. Evaluation Metrics: Two Disciplines That Must Not Collapse

*In this chapter, production observability and LLM evaluation are established as structurally distinct disciplines: different questions, different methods, different owners, different timescales. The chapter names the boundary (the Eval/Ops Split), traces the consequences of ignoring it through a documented quality regression, maps the overlap and divergence zones, examines the SLO problem for non-deterministic systems, and identifies the organizational ownership models that resolve the confusion. The principle-level boundary established here is what makes the Datadog-specific implementation choices in Part IV meaningful.*


A wealth advisory chatbot at a mid-size financial services company stopped performing well in early 2025. Customer satisfaction scores declined sharply over three weeks; the fraction of conversations ending with a user thumbs-down climbed from approximately 12 percent to over 28 percent.<sup>[1]</sup>

The platform engineering team opened Datadog. P95 latency: 4.2 seconds, well within the configured SLO of 8 seconds. Error rate on the LLM spans: 0.3 percent, within tolerance. Token consumption per session: slightly elevated but within expected range. Infrastructure health: green across every dimension. The dashboards told a story of a healthy, performant service.

The product team had been collecting something different. A domain expert from the wealth advisory group spent two weeks reviewing sampled conversations, scoring outputs for factual accuracy, appropriate product citations, and regulatory compliance. The fraction of responses citing the wrong fund products had tripled. The fraction of responses containing outdated yield figures had nearly doubled. The model was producing answers that were confident, grammatically correct, well-structured, and wrong in ways that could expose the firm to regulatory liability. The LLM had not broken. It had drifted. The drift was invisible to every operational tool the platform team had in place.<sup>[1]</sup>

The two teams spent another two weeks investigating in parallel before a joint postmortem produced the insight that should have been established at system design time: they were asking different questions, using different methods, producing incompatible results, and routing those findings to the same group of people who had no framework for adjudicating between them. One team was asking whether the system was healthy. The other was asking whether the model was performing well. These are different questions. They have different answers. Neither team had a name for the distinction, and that absence was, structurally, the problem.


What makes that wealth advisory team's situation relevant to this chapter is not the drift itself. Model drift is a known phenomenon, documented in the academic literature and in practitioner accounts stretching back to the pre-LLM era of ML deployment. The relevant point is that the two teams' parallel investigation represented a structural confusion that a well-designed system would have prevented: the conflation of two fundamentally distinct measurement disciplines, each with its own questions, methods, toolchains, and ownership patterns. The conflation imposed two weeks of wasted investigative effort. It produced a postmortem that blamed monitoring inadequacy when the actual failure was organizational: the wrong team was looking at the wrong signals for the wrong problem.

**The Eval/Ops Split.** The principled separation between production observability (is the system healthy?) and LLM evaluation (is the model performing well?), two disciplines that answer different questions, require different methods, must not be conflated in toolchain design or team ownership, and cannot be resolved by running more of either discipline's tools on the other discipline's questions. The Eval/Ops Split is not a claim about which discipline matters more. It is a claim about what each discipline can and cannot see, and about the organizational and toolchain consequences of treating them as a single coherent practice when they are not.

## Two Questions, Two Methods

The operational question has a long and well-developed answer in the observability literature. It asks about latency distributions, error rates, availability percentages, resource utilization, and dependency health. These signals are mechanical. A span either closes with a 200 status or it does not. A P99 latency is either within the SLO threshold or it is not. A pod either has available CPU or it does not. The signal is binary or continuous in a mechanically deterministic way. The measurement apparatus (a tracing library, a metrics collector, a log ingestion pipeline) records physical facts about the system's computational behavior. Given the same measurement event, the same apparatus will produce the same reading. The determinism is built into the instrument.

The evaluation question is structurally different in kind. It asks about semantic properties of model outputs: factual accuracy, logical consistency, appropriate tone, relevance to the user's actual intent, absence of harmful content, groundedness in the retrieved context, adherence to safety policy. These are not mechanical properties. A response's factual accuracy cannot be determined by reading a status code or measuring a duration. It requires comparison against an external standard of truth, which may itself be probabilistic, context-dependent, and contested. The measurement apparatus for evaluation is not a tracing library. It is a judge (human or LLM-based) applying rubrics developed by domain experts, calibrated against human rater agreement, and subject to its own drift over time.

This difference in measurement method has a direct implication for what counts as a valid signal. An operational metric carries an inherent guarantee of determinism: every measurement of the same event will produce the same result given the same apparatus. A latency of 4.2 seconds, measured by two different tracing libraries implementing the same specification, will agree within instrumentation error margins of fractions of a millisecond. An LLM evaluation score, produced by two different judges evaluating the same response, may disagree substantially even when both judges are well-calibrated. Phillip Carter, Principal PM at Honeycomb, documented this concretely in the published account of Honeycomb's Query Assistant SLO design: after multiple iterations of calibrating the LLM judge against human raters, the team achieved greater than 90 percent agreement, which they treated as acceptable for their context but which is still materially worse than the determinism of a mechanical measurement.<sup>[2]</sup>

Conflating operational metrics with evaluation metrics does not simply combine two kinds of signals in one place. It combines two fundamentally different epistemologies in a single dashboard or alert policy. The confusion runs in both directions.

The epistemological difference has a practical consequence for incident response. Operational signals are designed to be actionable immediately. A P99 latency alert that fires at 2 a.m. carries an implicit claim that the system is in a state requiring intervention now, and the SRE who receives it can verify or refute that claim within minutes using the trace backend. Evaluation signals are designed to be actionable over a longer window. A decline in hallucination rate over a seven-day rolling average carries an implicit claim that the model's behavior has shifted in a way that requires investigation by the coalition of domain experts, ML engineers, and product managers who together own the quality rubric. The SRE who receives that signal at 2 a.m. cannot verify or refute it without the domain expertise and evaluation toolchain that the coalition maintains. The signal is correct and urgent, but it is not operationally urgent. The appropriate response is investigation the following business day, not immediate incident escalation.

This is why the most experienced practitioners, including Carter at Honeycomb, Austin Parker at Honeycomb, and the Applied-LLMs collective, have converged on routing evaluation-layer alerts to low-urgency channels rather than to incident response queues.<sup>[2]</sup> The routing decision is not a statement about the importance of quality. It is a statement about who can respond to a quality alert with useful action at 2 a.m. The answer, consistently, is nobody with the right context. The Eval/Ops Split is, among other things, a routing discipline.

When an SRE team is paged on a quality regression signaled through the operational channel, they arrive at the incident with the wrong tools. They know how to look at traces, examine latency distributions, check error rates, and correlate infrastructure anomalies with application behavior. They do not have the domain expertise to judge whether a particular response to a wealth advisory question contained an outdated yield figure. They do not have access to the ground-truth dataset that would allow them to score factual accuracy. They cannot determine from a trace whether the retrieved documents were relevant to the query. The alert reached the right toolchain. It reached the wrong team, equipped with the wrong methods, for the wrong problem.

The reverse failure is equally common and equally costly. When a machine learning or product team investigating a suspected quality regression focuses exclusively on model output scores without checking operational metrics, they may spend weeks chasing a quality degradation whose proximate cause is an operational change: a retrieval configuration update that changed which documents were injected into the prompt context, an embedding model update that quietly altered the coordinate space of the vector index, a context-length limit that began truncating the system prompt under higher-than-expected load, a token budget change that caused the model to produce shorter responses. All of these operational changes produce quality-looking regressions in the evaluation layer. None of them are findable by examining evaluation scores alone. The model has not changed. The pipeline has. The evaluation team chases the wrong signal.

## Where Both Disciplines Converge

The Eval/Ops Split is not a claim that operational signals and evaluation signals have no relationship. There is a band of signals where they converge, and understanding that band is as important as understanding where they diverge. The disciplines share evidence at the edges. They do not share methods or epistemological standing anywhere in the shared zone; they simply happen to measure phenomena that are simultaneously mechanical and semantic.

The following table maps the primary signal categories across both disciplines. It identifies which team owns the signal, what measurement cadence applies, and whether the signal is deterministic or probabilistic.

| Signal Category | Operational (SRE-owned) | Evaluation (Domain-expert-owned) | Measurement Character |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Latency (P50, P95, P99, TTFT) | Real-time; SLO burn-rate alerts | User-experience quality proxy at extremes | Deterministic |
| Error rate (HTTP 4xx/5xx, provider errors) | Real-time; incident-response trigger | "No useful output" event in eval log | Deterministic |
| Structural correctness (JSON parse, schema validate, query execute) | Real-time; boolean SLI | First-line quality gate for structured output | Deterministic |
| Hallucination / factual accuracy | Not visible (HTTP 200, clean span) | Sampled LLM-as-judge or human review; multi-day trend | Probabilistic |
| Toxicity / safety policy compliance | Not visible (clean trace) | Automated evaluator templates; low-urgency routing | Probabilistic |
| Retrieval relevance | Call succeeded, results returned, latency within budget | Semantic appropriateness of retrieved documents to query | Probabilistic |
| Distribution / embedding drift | Absent from operational telemetry | Statistical divergence from historical baseline; leading indicator | Probabilistic |
| Service drift (silent provider-side model update) | Model identifier unchanged; no operational signal | Evaluation-score drift under stable API endpoint | Probabilistic |

Latency is the most obvious point of convergence. An LLM that takes 30 seconds to respond to a simple factual query is both operationally degraded (the latency SLO is violated) and evaluatively degraded (the response arrived too late to be useful, a form of quality failure regardless of semantic content). Bloomberg's multi-cluster generative AI inference platform, documented in a KubeCon talk series by Alexa Griffith and Sal Furino, explicitly treats time-to-first-token, tokens per output token, token throughput, and queue depth as first-class SLO dimensions alongside standard latency and error-rate metrics.<sup>[2]</sup> At the latency extremes, the two disciplines agree: a response that never arrives is both an operational error and an evaluation failure.

Error rates carry the same dual character. A hard failure in the LLM API call (a 429 rate limit or a 503 provider error) is an operational event recorded by the trace. It is also an evaluation event in the sense that the system produced no useful output. Teams that maintain dual recording can distinguish between *the system failed to respond at all* and *the system responded with content that was factually incorrect*, which are meaningfully different failure categories even though both register as a quality regression from the user's perspective.

Structural correctness is the most actionable convergence zone. When an LLM system produces structured output (a JSON object, a YAML configuration block, a SQL query, a function call with typed parameters), the output's structural validity is mechanically determinable. A JSON parse failure is an operational event. Schema validation failure is deterministic. Whether the parsed JSON contains semantically correct values is an evaluation question, but whether it parses at all is not. Carter's SLO design for the Query Assistant formalized this insight as the first line of SLI design: define any failure in the set of API call, output parse, schema validate, and downstream query execution as a boolean false, and build the SLO on top of those boolean outcomes.<sup>[2]</sup> This converts the non-deterministic question of *was the query good?* into the deterministic sub-question of *did the query parse and execute without error?* The pattern generalizes. LinkedIn constrains its routing LLM to emit YAML and treats schema-validation failures as deterministic error events, converting a model-quality signal into a mechanical signal that can support a traditional SLO.<sup>[2]</sup> Shopify's Global Catalogue routes outputs with low joint log-probability to human review rather than serving them as responses, treating model uncertainty as a structural correctness signal.<sup>[2]</sup>

The structural correctness layer belongs to both disciplines simultaneously, and it is the most defensible layer for operational alerting on AI systems. It does not require a judge. It does not require calibration against human raters. It requires only a deterministic boundary that the model's output must cross: parse, validate, execute. Everything that crosses the boundary is operationally successful. The semantic quality of outputs that cross the boundary is an evaluation question, addressed by the evaluation toolchain, owned by the evaluation coalition, and answered on evaluation timescales.

## Where the Disciplines Separate

Beyond the overlap band, the two disciplines diverge in ways that make their conflation structurally costly.

Hallucination and factual accuracy live entirely in the evaluation domain. There is no operational signal for a hallucination. A response that confidently asserts a fictional regulatory requirement, or cites a fund's five-year return using last year's figures, or attributes a quote to a person who never said it, will produce an HTTP 200 status, a successful span, a token count within the normal range, and a latency within the SLO. The operational toolchain records a successful transaction. The hallucination is invisible until a human or a properly calibrated evaluator reads the output and compares it against a source of truth.

The academic literature on this structural limitation is unambiguous. Two 2024 papers establish from different theoretical frameworks that hallucination rates in LLM systems cannot be driven to zero by construction: Banerjee et al. argues from learning theory that any model trained on finite data will have non-zero probability of generating false statements, and Xu et al. argues from undecidability theory that the ground-truth oracle required to catch all hallucinations is computationally intractable.<sup>[3,4]</sup> The implication for SLO design is that a zero-error-budget policy for hallucination is not a tractable operational target. The practitioner community has absorbed this finding. The Applied-LLMs collective, which includes Eugene Yan, Hamel Husain, Jason Liu, Shreya Shankar, Bryan Bischof, and Charles Frye, reports the observed production floor as a 5 to 10 percent baseline hallucination rate, with a hard floor around 2 percent even on simple summarization tasks, citing LLM provider data.<sup>[2]</sup>

Honeycomb's Query Assistant is the most thoroughly published public case of a team confronting this reality explicitly. The initial success-rate target was 75 percent, with Carter's account making explicit: *"Most services have a 95% or higher success target, but we felt that didn't make sense for the initial release of a feature using an LLM."*<sup>[2]</sup> His operational rule of thumb for teams starting an LLM quality SLO: *"Start with 50% or higher as your success rate, but don't go higher than 90%."*<sup>[2]</sup> That target reflects an accurate description of what the measurement can support, not a failure of ambition. The error budget for a quality SLO is structurally one to two orders of magnitude looser than for an availability SLO. A team that attempts to apply traditional web-service SLO targets to LLM quality metrics will find that the SLO fires constantly, becomes noise, and gets ignored. The lesson is not to set looser availability SLOs for AI services. It is to accept that the quality SLO lives in a different regime with different budget expectations, owned by a different team, answered by different methods.

Toxicity, tone, and safety policy compliance are similarly evaluation-only territory. A response that violates the company's content policy produces the same operational trace as a response that complies with it. Automated evaluators (specifically models fine-tuned or prompted to detect harmful content, bias, or policy violations) are the only toolchain that can flag these. Datadog ships out-of-the-box evaluation templates for toxicity, sentiment, and prompt injection as managed evaluators in LLM Observability (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[5]</sup> These templates run as automated LLM-as-judge evaluations against sampled production traces. The results flow into the LLM Observability dashboard as scored events alongside the operational trace data. The functional gap is that Datadog does not publish opinionated targets for these scores: the metric exists; the policy for what constitutes an acceptable toxicity score or hallucination rate is left to the operator. No vendor publishes a quantitative industry-consensus target for a hallucination or groundedness rate at the SLO level (per the SLOs research corpus, Q2 2026). The instrumentation is maturing faster than the policy framework around it.<sup>[2,5]</sup>

Retrieval quality sits at the operational-evaluation boundary in a way that illuminates the Split precisely. Whether retrieved documents are semantically relevant to the query is an evaluation question. Whether the retrieval call succeeded, returned the configured number of results, and completed within the latency budget are operational questions. A team that monitors only the operational signals from the retrieval layer knows whether the call succeeded. It does not know whether the documents it returned were the right documents. The retrieval layer's latency, error rate, and response code are operational signals. Its relevance scores, document freshness, and semantic appropriateness to the query are evaluation signals. The two signal types require different instruments, different ownership, and different response playbooks.

Distribution drift occupies a middle zone that the community has increasingly brought into the evaluation toolchain. Maria Vechtomova, speaking at SRECon EMEA 2025, articulated the core insight: *"The statistical properties of the data can change, and your model stops performing. That's what happened during COVID: forecasting and recommender systems broke because we had never seen that kind of data before."*<sup>[6]</sup> Distribution drift alerts on the absence of evidence (the divergence of the current query distribution from the historical baseline) rather than on the presence of explicit failures. WhyLabs built its product architecture around whylogs-based drift profiles (per WhyLabs product documentation, accessed Q2 2026). Arize Phoenix provides embedding drift visualization. Dynatrace categorizes model drift and data drift as named observability concerns alongside standard operational metrics. This drift detection work is evaluation-adjacent: it does not measure the quality of individual responses, but it provides early warning that the system is operating in territory where its calibrated behavior may no longer apply.

Service drift is a closely related phenomenon documented in recent academic literature.<sup>[7]</sup> Hosted LLM providers make silent updates to the model behind a stable API endpoint. The provider does not announce the update. The model identifier in the span remains unchanged. The operational telemetry shows no disruption. The evaluation scores begin to drift because the model's behavior has shifted in ways not reflected in any operational signal. The appropriate monitoring response (pinning model versions where the provider supports it and treating unannounced behavior shifts detected through evaluation-score drift as an alert condition) is an evaluation-layer practice with no operational-layer analog.

## The Judge Reliability Problem

The evaluation discipline has its own measurement reliability challenge, one that is distinct from both the hallucination problem and the distribution drift problem and that the operational discipline does not face. The judge (the LLM-as-judge evaluator that scores production responses against a rubric) is itself a non-deterministic system whose reliability must be monitored and maintained. An LLM-as-judge calibrated against human raters in January 2025 will, by June 2025, have drifted in its rubric application in ways that may not be visible without re-calibration.

The rubric drift problem is documented empirically. A 2026 paper titled *Rubrics as an Attack Surface* demonstrated that harmlessness accuracy in a calibrated judge dropped 27.9 percent under adversarial pressure, without any change to the judge's surface-level behavior.<sup>[8]</sup> A 2025 paper from Wells Fargo researchers, *Who Judges the Judge?*, documented that individual LLM judges hallucinate their own evaluations roughly one in ten times, producing a noise floor in any single-judge SLO that cannot be reduced by lowering alert thresholds or increasing metric sampling frequency.<sup>[9]</sup>

These findings have a direct implication for teams building evaluation-layer SLOs. A quality SLO built on the output of a single, uncalibrated judge is not a stable measurement instrument. It is a system with its own drift characteristics and its own failure modes. The meta-problem of monitoring the judge is structurally the same as the problem of monitoring the model: both are non-deterministic systems whose behavior can change without producing operational signals.

The practitioner community has developed three mitigations, none of which fully resolves the problem. First, quorum-of-judges approaches, exemplified by the Wells Fargo *LLM Jury-on-Demand* paper, run multiple judges on the same response and require agreement before treating a low score as an alert condition.<sup>[9]</sup> This reduces noise at the cost of latency and inference expense. Second, periodic human-anchored re-calibration, with the calibration interval informed by the rate of model drift in the judge itself, provides a periodic ground-truth reset. The Applied-LLMs collective explicitly recommends *"look at samples every day"* as part of on-call rotations, using what they call the *"intern test"*: if a human intern in the relevant domain could grade the output given the same context, the task is fair to evaluate, and a sampled human review pipeline provides the ground truth against which the judge is calibrated.<sup>[2]</sup> Third, score smoothing (running multiple evaluations on the same response and taking the average or the majority vote) reduces the impact of individual judge responses that fall outside the judge's calibrated distribution.

The evaluation discipline requires more maintenance than the operational discipline. A latency SLO, once configured correctly, continues to fire correctly indefinitely without recalibration. A quality SLO requires ongoing calibration work to remain a reliable measurement. Teams that treat the evaluation layer as a one-time configuration will find that the SLO's reliability degrades over time in ways that are difficult to detect without explicit monitoring of the judge itself. This is not a reason to avoid evaluation-layer SLOs. It is a reason to staff and operate them with the understanding that the measurement apparatus requires the same ongoing investment as the system it is measuring.

## The SLO Problem for Non-Deterministic Systems

The SLO framework, as developed by the Google Site Reliability Engineering group and formalized in the SRE Workbook, was designed for systems whose error budget can be defined against a binary oracle: a request either succeeded or it failed, and the budget tracks the fraction of failures over a rolling window.<sup>[10]</sup> The burn-rate alert fires when the failure fraction consumes the budget faster than the window can replenish it. This framework is highly functional for operational metrics, where the oracle is mechanical and the failure definition is unambiguous.

For evaluation metrics, the oracle problem is not mechanical. There is no ground truth available in real time for most quality signals. The system cannot know, at the moment of serving a response, whether that response is factually accurate without running a separate evaluation that is itself probabilistic. The burn-rate alert framework, applied directly to evaluation scores, must therefore operate on an SLI that is itself a sample, produced by a non-deterministic judge, with measurement error one to two orders of magnitude larger than a mechanical failure count.

The community's response is instructive, because it has converged on a practical decomposition rather than waiting for a theoretical resolution. The defensible pattern separates the SLO into two layers. The first layer applies burn-rate alerting only to the deterministic sub-checks: schema validation failures, downstream execution errors, hard API failures, structural correctness. These have real boolean signals and can support a traditional error-budget framework with normal alert thresholds and escalation policies. The second layer applies anomaly detection and distribution monitoring to the evaluation scores, watching for sustained directional drift over windows long enough to absorb the noise in individual judge evaluations, rather than firing alerts on individual score violations.<sup>[2]</sup>

Carter's design for the Query Assistant used a seven-day SLO window with a Slack notification four hours before budget exhaustion, and explicitly rejected PagerDuty alerting for LLM quality failures on the grounds that *"a rogue user who intentionally tries to get your feature to mess up through prompt injection"* should not wake someone up at 2 a.m.<sup>[2]</sup> The distinction between a Slack notification and a PagerDuty page reflects a principled decision about which failure modes require immediate human intervention and which require investigation on the next business day. Operational failures (a provider outage, a latency spike, a hard error rate exceeding the budget) require immediate response. Quality regressions (a gradual drift in hallucination rate, a trend toward lower relevance scores) require investigation but not interruption. The alert policy encodes the discipline's epistemological standing.

Austin Parker of Honeycomb extended the burn-rate framework to agentic systems in an April 2026 post: *"If an agent is making code changes that technically pass tests but cause a gradual SLO burn, you need that burn rate alert to fire and trigger a review. The agent doesn't need to know it's drifting; your observability platform does."*<sup>[11]</sup> The *"gradual SLO burn"* Parker describes is a burn-rate alert applied to the structural correctness layer. The tests that pass are the evaluation layer's deterministic sub-checks. The drift that the observability platform detects is distribution drift in the evaluation signals. All three operate simultaneously, but they are organized and owned differently.

Denys Vasyliev's framing in *AI Reliability Engineering: Welcome to the Third Age of SRE*, published in The New Stack in June 2025, captures the conceptual reframe that the Split requires: *"Unlike crashing pods or failing endpoints, silent model degradation slips under the radar. The model keeps responding, but its answers grow weaker, biased, or just wrong. Users don't see 500 errors; they get hallucinations, toxic outputs, or faulty decisions. In the world of AI, correctness is uptime. When reliability means quality, degradation is downtime."*<sup>[12]</sup> The reframe does not collapse the two disciplines. It redefines what each discipline's failure mode looks like in the AI context, and it assigns *correctness* to the evaluation discipline rather than to the operational discipline, precisely because correctness cannot be measured mechanically.

The EU AI Act brings a regulatory dimension to this framework that is material for teams deploying AI in regulated industries. Articles 9 and 17 of the Act require providers of high-risk AI systems (as defined in Annex III) to establish risk management systems and implement a post-market monitoring plan that tracks the system's performance throughout its lifecycle (EU AI Act, in force August 2024).<sup>[13]</sup> The monitoring obligation extends beyond uptime and error rates to include the system's ability to perform its intended purpose accurately. The Act does not prescribe specific technical standards for what evaluation monitoring must include; the EU AI Office's implementation guidance was still in development as of Q2 2026. But the obligation creates a legal context in which the Eval/Ops Split is not merely a best-practice distinction. For systems classified as high-risk under Annex III (which includes AI systems used in critical infrastructure, educational qualification, employment decisions, essential services access, law enforcement, migration management, administration of justice, and democratic processes), the post-market monitoring plan must address evaluation signals alongside operational signals. A compliance team that attempts to satisfy this obligation using only the operational monitoring layer is not meeting the Act's intent, even if the dashboard shows green.

## The Ownership Problem

The Eval/Ops Split has an organizational analog that is, in practice, harder to resolve than the toolchain separation. Operational metrics are owned by the SRE or platform team. The SRE team has the context, the toolchain access, and the on-call rotation to respond to operational alerts. Evaluation metrics are owned, in the published accounts of the teams that have solved this well, by a coalition: domain experts who define the rubric, ML engineers who implement and calibrate the judge, and product teams who set the acceptable quality thresholds.

This coalition does not have a PagerDuty rotation. It does not have an incident response playbook in the SRE sense. The work the coalition does is not incident response work. It is continuous improvement work: reviewing sampled outputs, refining rubrics, calibrating judges against human rater agreement, tracking quality trends over weeks rather than minutes. Hamel Husain's field guide to LLM evaluation states the ownership principle directly: *"Empower domain experts to write prompts and define eval criteria."*<sup>[14]</sup> Carter's Honeycomb account is the clearest case study: a Principal Product Manager wrote the Query Assistant prompts, designed the user-feedback schema, calibrated the LLM judge through three iterations, and reached more than 90 percent agreement with human raters. An SRE did not own that work. A machine learning engineer did not own that work. The domain expert who understood what a correct query assistant response looked like owned it.<sup>[2]</sup>

Practitioner accounts document three ownership models that work at different organizational scales, and a failure mode that appears consistently when none of them are in place.

The PM-led model, exemplified by Honeycomb, works at small-to-medium scale where the product manager has direct domain expertise and technical literacy. The PM owns the rubric, the feedback schema, and the judge calibration. SRE owns the burn-rate alert infrastructure. ML engineering provides toolchain support. This model breaks down when the domain expertise required to define the rubric is concentrated in specialists who are not product managers, as in the wealth advisory case where the rubric required regulatory and financial product knowledge that no PM had.

The dedicated evaluation team model, employed at Anthropic per remarks made by Tom Brown in January 2024, involves a team fully focused on reducing hallucination and factual error rates, with dedicated headcount and infrastructure.<sup>[15]</sup> This model scales to the frontier lab environment but is not accessible to most product teams building LLM-backed applications.

The platform-engineering-led model, documented in Bloomberg's KubeCon series and in the CNCF's emerging platformengineering.org community, assigns retrieval freshness, embedding-index health, PII filtering, and rate-limited tool wrappers to the platform team as shared infrastructure SLOs. The platform team does not own the output quality rubric, but it owns the infrastructure that makes quality measurement possible, including the token-level rate limiting, distributed inference routing, and observability hooks that Bloomberg's Envoy AI Gateway layer provides.<sup>[2]</sup> This model is attractive for organizations with multiple teams building on a shared AI infrastructure, because it distributes the evaluation ownership to the feature teams who understand their specific domain while centralizing the measurement infrastructure.

What these accounts document clearly is the failure mode when none of these models is in place: two teams operating with incompatible measurement frameworks, no organizational structure for adjudicating conflicts between them, and no named boundary that tells each team where its responsibility ends and the other's begins. The absence of the Eval/Ops Split as an explicit organizational construct is itself the failure. The resolution, in every documented case, begins with naming the boundary and assigning clear ownership of each side.

## The Silent Regression, the Over-Instrumentation Trap, and the Service-Drift Blind Spot

The Eval/Ops Split manifests in three recurring patterns that each carry specific toolchain and ownership implications.

The silent regression pattern is the most dangerous failure mode in AI systems that monitor only the operational layer. A model or retrieval component degrades in a way that produces no operational signal. The traces are clean. The error rates are flat. The latency is within the SLO. The evaluation scores, if they are being collected, decline steadily. The pattern is structurally invisible to all operational alerting. Charity Majors' formulation in her March 2026 post on agent observability captures the mechanism precisely: *"If you are trying to do anomaly detection with no a priori knowledge of what to look for, the anomaly has to be fairly large to be detected. But if you do have some knowledge of intent, along with precision tooling, these anomalies can be tracked and validated even when they are exquisitely minute."*<sup>[16]</sup> The *"knowledge of intent"* is the evaluation rubric. The *"precision tooling"* is the calibrated judge. Without both, the silent regression proceeds until a user or a human reviewer notices.

The over-instrumentation pattern is the organizational response that looks correct and still fails. A team routes all evaluation signals into the operational monitoring platform, creating a dashboard where latency SLOs, error rates, hallucination scores, toxicity flags, retrieval relevance scores, and user satisfaction ratings appear side by side without organizational differentiation. The dashboard is complete. It is also unactionable, because the signals have different noise characteristics, different measurement methods, different ownership implications, and different appropriate response playbooks. An SRE arriving at this dashboard during an incident cannot distinguish which signal is operationally urgent from which is evaluatively concerning. A hallucination score decline requires routing to the domain-expert coalition for rubric re-evaluation and model assessment; a latency spike requires the SRE's standard incident response workflow. The solution is not to remove the evaluation signals from view. It is to configure them with the ownership and alert policies appropriate to the evaluation discipline: slow-burn trend alerts routed to the evaluation coalition, with a notification protocol that does not page the SRE on-call for quality signals that require domain-expert interpretation.

The service-drift blind spot involves hosted LLM providers making silent updates to the model behind a stable API endpoint.<sup>[7]</sup> The provider does not announce the update. The model identifier in the span remains unchanged. The operational telemetry shows no disruption. The evaluation scores begin to drift because the model's behavior has changed in ways not reflected in any operational signal. The Slack AI indirect data exfiltration incident from 2024 illustrates a related class of provider-side change: a feature update that introduced new attack surfaces while preserving operational health signals.<sup>[17]</sup> Between August and October 2024, the system continued to function correctly from an operational perspective. From an evaluation perspective, a new attack class had become possible that the operational toolchain could not detect without a specifically configured security evaluation layer. The appropriate monitoring response is an evaluation-layer practice: treating unannounced behavior shifts detected through evaluation-score drift as an alert condition rather than normal variation.

## The Toolchain Division

The toolchain implications of the Eval/Ops Split are straightforward to state at the principle level and organizationally difficult to implement consistently. The Datadog-specific implementation (including the LLM Observability evaluation surface, the LLM Experiments SDK, the Annotation Queues feature, and the out-of-the-box evaluator templates) is treated in Chapter 7 as part of the broader treatment of **The Datadog AI Surface**.

The operational toolchain is structured around a time-series database, a trace backend, and an alerting engine. Its primary interface is a dashboard designed for real-time incident response. Its alerting policy is configured for fast detection and fast escalation. Its primary consumers are SREs and platform engineers who need to determine, in minutes, whether the system needs immediate intervention.

The evaluation toolchain is structured around a dataset of sampled interactions, a judge pipeline that scores those interactions against a rubric, and an analytics layer that tracks score distributions over time. Its primary interface is a quality review dashboard designed for trend analysis rather than real-time monitoring. Its alerting policy, where one exists, is configured for slow-burn detection over multi-day windows rather than for fast escalation. Its primary consumers are ML engineers, product managers, and domain experts who need to determine, over days or weeks, whether the model's behavior is drifting.

These toolchains can share infrastructure, and the most mature production implementations do share infrastructure at the trace storage layer. A trace backend that stores full interaction payloads (including prompts and responses) enables both operational span analysis and offline evaluation dataset construction from the same underlying store. This is the architectural pattern that Datadog LLM Observability implements: production traces are stored in the LLM Observability backend, and evaluation datasets can be constructed from those stored traces using the LLM Experiments SDK, with human annotation possible through the Annotation Queues feature (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[5]</sup> The shared trace storage layer is the bridge between the two disciplines, not a collapse of them. The evaluation pipeline reads from the same store as the operational toolchain. It runs asynchronously, at sampled rates, with its own scoring logic and its own result store.

Where specialized evaluation tools hold their structural advantage is in capabilities that an operational monitoring platform was not designed to provide: prompt version management, structured human annotation queues with multi-reviewer aggregation logic, dataset versioning and ground-truth labeling workflows, CI/CD blocking gates on quality regression, and the ability to run controlled comparisons between two model versions on the same evaluation dataset before a deployment decision. Specialized evaluation tools (Langfuse, LangSmith, Braintrust, Arize Phoenix) provide human annotation queues and CI/CD quality gates not available natively in Datadog as of Q2 2026 (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation and Gemini Architectural Evolution report, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[5,18]</sup> These are evaluation-discipline features that reflect a different product philosophy: the primary consumer of the toolchain is the team deciding whether to promote a model version to production, not the team managing a 2 a.m. incident.

Datadog LLM Observability's evaluation surface, as of Q2 2026, provides online evaluation with out-of-the-box judge templates for hallucination, failure-to-answer, topic relevancy, toxicity, sentiment, goal completeness, tool selection, tool argument correctness, and prompt injection, alongside the LLM Experiments SDK for offline evaluation against archived datasets (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[5]</sup> The Annotation Queues feature provides a structured workflow for human review: queues can be populated automatically from production traces using Automation Rules that filter by evaluation results, latency thresholds, or application, routing matching traces at up to 5 percent sampling to a named annotation queue. Human reviewers apply structured labels with multi-reviewer aggregation logic for disagreement resolution, and completed annotations can be exported for dataset construction or CI/CD integration. The maximum queue size of 1,000 records is a documented constraint that reflects the difference between an annotation tool embedded in an operational platform and a purpose-built annotation system designed for large-scale ground-truth labeling (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[5]</sup> Teams running high-volume evaluation pipelines will reach this limit and need to architect a rotation strategy or supplement with a specialized evaluation tool.

The documented structural gap in Datadog's evaluation surface, as of Q2 2026, is in the pre-deployment blocking gate. Datadog does not provide a native mechanism for blocking a code merge or a model deployment when evaluation scores regress below a configured threshold (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[5]</sup> That gap belongs to the evaluation discipline as the mechanism for preventing quality regressions from reaching production. The operational platform can record that quality has degraded after a deployment; it cannot prevent the deployment from occurring based on pre-deployment quality evaluation. The resolution at the organizational level is to connect the evaluation toolchain (whether Datadog's LLM Experiments or a specialized tool) to the CI/CD pipeline outside of Datadog.

The assessment here is that the Eval/Ops Split is not an argument for two separate platforms in every case. It is an argument for two separate disciplines, with clear ownership boundaries, clear method boundaries, and clear toolchain boundaries that may or may not map to separate vendor products depending on the team's scale and requirements. A team with low evaluation volume and a platform-centric organizational model may route both disciplines through a single platform and accept the tradeoffs. A team operating at the scale of a Bloomberg AI platform, a Shopify Sidekick, or a Honeycomb Query Assistant, where evaluation is a continuous engineering practice with its own team, its own release gates, and its own alerting policy, requires the separation to be structural and explicit. The important variable is not the number of platforms. It is whether the boundary between the two disciplines has been named, documented, and assigned to specific owners.


The wealth advisory postmortem took five weeks to resolve what a well-designed system would have caught in five days.

## Key Points

- **The Eval/Ops Split** separates two questions requiring different methods, different teams, and different alert routing: system health and model performance.
- Structural correctness (parse, validate, execute) is the strongest operational SLO foundation because it converts non-deterministic quality into deterministic boolean signals.
- Hallucination, factual accuracy, toxicity, and retrieval relevance are invisible to operational monitoring; each requires a separate judge pipeline with distinct ownership.
- Quality SLOs need error budgets one to two orders of magnitude looser than availability SLOs; traditional thresholds produce permanent noise on non-deterministic output.
- The LLM-as-judge drifts; teams that skip periodic human-anchored re-calibration hold a measurement instrument that silently degrades.
- The EU AI Act's post-market monitoring obligation for high-risk systems requires evaluation signals; operational monitoring alone does not satisfy it.
