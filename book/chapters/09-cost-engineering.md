# Chapter 9: Cost Engineering

*In this chapter: Datadog's billing architecture applies five independent cost dimensions to every AI workload simultaneously, and the interaction effects compound in ways that no single-pillar cost review will catch. The chapter traces token-cost attribution from span-level estimates through provider-confirmed actuals, examines documented overruns and the configuration levers that resolved them, and develops the engineering discipline that makes observability cost tractable before the first invoice arrives.*

In October 2024, the engineering team at a cloud communications company discovered that their Datadog invoice had become the second-largest line item in their infrastructure budget. Not the second-largest observability line item. The second-largest infrastructure line item overall, approaching the cost of the compute fleet the observability stack was monitoring. The company engaged a consultancy for a six-week cost optimization engagement. The consultants found a pattern that had by then become familiar across dozens of similar engagements: the team had added an LLM-backed feature six months earlier, instrumented it thoroughly using the same tagging practices they applied to their traditional services, and never revisited the configuration once the feature launched. In those six months, custom metric cardinality had expanded by a factor of roughly eighteen, log ingestion had tripled, and indexed span counts had climbed proportionally with the growth of the AI feature's user base. None of these increases were individually anomalous. The aggregate effect was a bill that had grown approximately $112,000 per year beyond what the baseline infrastructure growth would have predicted. The six-week engagement addressed the problem through a combination of retention tuning, index restructuring, and metric configuration changes. The bill returned to a manageable level.<sup>[1]</sup>

The number that matters is not the $112,000. It is the six months. For half a year the team had a cost structure growing faster than the product, and the monitoring stack that was supposed to surface anomalies had nothing to surface, because each individual component of the bill was operating within its configured limits. No individual metric was wrong. No individual index was over quota. The aggregate compounding was invisible until it arrived on an invoice.

What makes that communications company's situation relevant here is what it reveals about the structural difference between cost awareness and cost governance. The team was not unaware of their Datadog costs. They received invoices monthly. They reviewed dashboards. They had monitors on individual usage metrics. What they lacked was a framework for understanding how different billing dimensions interacted, how a new AI feature would affect each dimension simultaneously, and how to configure the instrumentation layer to capture diagnostic value without accepting worst-case billing implications.

This chapter is about that framework. It is also the chapter where the engineering discipline this book calls **Token-Cost Attribution** finds its full treatment.

**Token-Cost Attribution.** The engineering practice of connecting token consumption recorded at the individual span level to a fully loaded cost figure assigned to a business transaction, user session, or organizational unit, making the economic consequence of each LLM call attributable rather than aggregate.

Token-cost attribution is not a product feature. It is a practice that teams build from three inputs that do not arrive in the same system: span-level token counts from the instrumentation layer, estimated per-token costs derived from published provider pricing, and provider-confirmed actuals retrieved through billing APIs. The gap between those three inputs, and the engineering required to close it, is the cost engineering problem that this chapter develops.

## The Billing Architecture Under AI Workloads

Understanding why AI workloads compound Datadog costs requires understanding the billing architecture at a layer of specificity that the pricing page does not make immediately obvious.

Datadog's cost model has five active dimensions that all apply simultaneously to an instrumented AI application: APM host fees, indexed span volume, ingested span volume overage, custom metric time series, and log management fees combining ingestion and indexing charges. The per-dimension rates, as documented in the Datadog APM billing documentation and confirmed by multiple independent analyses, are: APM at $31 per host per month at the base tier, with one million indexed spans included per APM host per month and indexed span overages at $1.70 per million; ingested span overages at $0.10 per gigabyte beyond the included allocation; custom metrics at $0.05 per active time series per month for overages beyond the per-host inclusion; log ingestion at $0.10 per gigabyte; and log indexing at $1.70 per million events for 15-day retention and $2.55 per million events for 30-day retention (per Datadog APM Billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[2]</sup>

In isolation, each rate is modest. In combination, for an AI workload, they interact in a way that traditional APM workloads rarely trigger. The interaction has three distinct amplifiers.

### Span Density

A traditional HTTP service generates roughly one to three spans per user request. An LLM call with a simple prompt-and-response pattern generates approximately three to ten spans through the span taxonomy that Datadog's classification assigns to each stage of the AI pipeline. A multi-step agent workflow generates hundreds to thousands of spans per session, with each tool invocation, retrieval step, and LLM call adding to the count.<sup>[3]</sup> At a span density ten to fifty times higher than traditional services, an AI workload consumes the indexed span inclusion with each APM host much faster than the APM billing model was designed to assume. A team that previously operated comfortably within the one-million-span-per-host monthly inclusion will find that same host budget exhausted in days when the workload switches to agent orchestration.

### Log Payload Size

AI workloads emit prompt and response payloads as log fields. A single multi-turn conversation with a 50,000-token context window can produce a log event several hundred kilobytes in size. A system handling ten thousand such conversations per day produces several gigabytes of AI-layer log data before any operational log, error log, or access log is counted. Log ingestion at $0.10 per gigabyte is modest for traditional log volumes but compounds quickly at the payload sizes that conversational AI systems produce. Log indexing compounds this further: at $1.70 per million events, a system that indexes full AI payloads at 15-day retention is paying for storage of conversational content at a rate that scales with both the number of users and the verbosity of each interaction.<sup>[3]</sup>

### The Cardinality Dimension

**The Cardinality Cliff**, the cost-compounding principle established in Chapter 3, appears in the AI workload context when instrumentation attaches any text derived from a prompt or response to a metric or indexed span tag. A token-count metric tagged with `model_name`, `environment`, and `service` has bounded cardinality; those dimensions have small, finite value sets. The same metric tagged with any field that includes session content, query prefixes, or document identifiers becomes unbounded. Datadog's billing model counts each unique combination of a metric name and its tag key-value pairs as a separate billable time series, at $0.05 per series per month for overages (per Datadog custom metrics billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[4]</sup> The multiplication is not gradual. A single high-cardinality tag transforms a metric with a bounded time series count into one that produces tens of thousands of series per hour.

### A Worked Example: Token-Cost Attribution for a Single Agent Session

A concrete example from the independent cost analysis corpus illustrates how these three amplifiers interact in a single deployment. Consider a 16-microservice application extended with an LLM recommendation service, instrumented via OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions. In a documented test scenario, that service generated only 232 LLM spans, yet those 232 spans triggered the LLM Observability product at a cost of approximately $120 per day in the test environment, forming the single largest cost line item in a total observability cost of roughly $174 per day for the full application stack. The LLM Observability component represented approximately 69 percent of total cost, generated by a service that represented a fraction of the application's overall traffic.<sup>[5]</sup>

To see how token-cost attribution works at the session level, trace the arithmetic for a single agentic session in a production customer-support system handling 50,000 user messages per day. Each user message generates approximately four LLM invocations (an initial classification call, a retrieval-augmented generation call, a response-quality evaluation call, and a summarization call). That yields roughly 200,000 LLM invocations per day. Each invocation produces on average 1,500 input tokens and 500 output tokens. At a blended rate of $3.00 per million input tokens and $15.00 per million output tokens (illustrative mid-tier rates, not sourced to a specific provider), the inference cost per invocation is approximately $0.012. Multiply across 200,000 daily invocations: roughly $2,400 per day, or approximately $72,000 per month in inference cost alone.<sup>[6]</sup>

The observability cost for the same workload sits alongside that figure. Those 200,000 daily invocations produce approximately one million spans per day (accounting for tool spans, retrieval spans, and embedding spans alongside the LLM-provider spans). One million spans per day is thirty million per month, consuming the indexed span inclusion for thirty APM hosts. The overage cost at $1.70 per million for spans beyond the inclusion, plus log ingestion for the conversational payloads at approximately 400 megabytes per day, plus custom metrics for token-count gauges and cost-attribution counters, can place the total observability cost between $129 and $5,170 per month depending on the vendor and configuration, with Datadog falling at the higher end of that range when full-fidelity tracing is left in place.<sup>[6]</sup> The ratio tells the story: at roughly $72,000 per month in inference cost and $129 to $5,170 per month in observability cost, observability represents approximately 0.2 to 7.2 percent of total AI system cost at this traffic volume. That ratio narrows sharply at lower traffic volumes and as model providers cut inference prices, which is the direction competitive pressure has moved since 2024.

The span-level cost estimate that makes token-cost attribution operational is derived from these same inputs. The instrumentation layer records `gen_ai.usage.input_tokens` and `gen_ai.usage.output_tokens` on each LLM span. The Datadog SDK or a post-processing pipeline multiplies those counts by the provider's published per-token rate for the model recorded in `gen_ai.request.model` and writes the result as a span attribute: estimated cost in dollars. That per-span estimate, summed across all spans sharing a session identifier, produces the session-level cost figure. Summed across all sessions tagged with a team or product identifier, it produces the organizational cost figure. The arithmetic is straightforward. The governance is not.

## The Billing Unit Change

The billing unit change from per-10,000-LLM-requests to per-LLM-span deserves its full treatment here, because it changes the billing surface from request-level granularity to span-level granularity. In agentic architectures the ratio of spans to requests can be in the hundreds or thousands. A billing model based on requests charges a flat amount per user-visible interaction, regardless of how many internal steps the agent took. A billing model based on spans charges for each step individually. A more capable agent, one doing more thorough work, using more tools, reasoning across more retrieval rounds, generates a larger bill than a simpler one handling the same user-visible request count. The financial incentive embedded in span-based billing runs counter to the architectural incentive to build more capable agents (per Datadog documentation and Claude research report, Q2 2026).<sup>[7]</sup>

The key clarification from Datadog's own documentation is that only LLM-provider spans are billed under the LLM Observability SKU. Tool spans, retrieval spans, and embedding spans generated by the same agentic session still consume APM ingestion and indexing capacity if APM is active, but they are not charged under the LLM Observability meter specifically. Custom metrics derived from span attributes hit the custom metrics meter regardless of source. Prompt and response payloads logged to the standard log store are charged as standard logs. The effective cost of fully instrumenting an AI workload is the sum of contributions across all five billing dimensions simultaneously, not the LLM Observability line item in isolation.<sup>[7]</sup>

For enterprise deployments, independent cost analyses arrive at ranges of $50,000 to $150,000 per year for the full-stack Datadog cost of monitoring a production AI application, with the exact figure depending on agent complexity, session volume, retention configuration, and negotiated enterprise pricing (per Vstorm comparative analysis, April 2026).<sup>[8]</sup> Teams monitoring AI workloads at scale without deliberate cost engineering routinely report 40 to 200 percent increases in their Datadog bills relative to the pre-AI baseline, driven by the combined effect of higher span density, larger log payloads, and cardinality expansion from AI-assisted instrumentation (per Tian Pan, "The Observability Tax," April 2026).<sup>[6]</sup>

## Cardinality Control in Practice

**The Cardinality Cliff** has a concrete Datadog-specific expression here: indexed tag cardinality limits per metric.

Datadog's billing model for custom metrics counts each unique combination of a metric name, host tag, and key-value tag pairs observed in a trailing hour as a separate billable time series. The per-plan inclusion is approximately 100 custom metric time series per host on the Pro plan, with overages at $5 per 100 additional series. These rates apply to all metrics not covered by standard platform integrations, which includes all OpenTelemetry-emitted metrics regardless of semantic content (per Datadog custom metrics billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[4]</sup> This has a structural implication for teams using the OTel GenAI conventions: every token-count gauge, operation-duration histogram, and cost-attribution counter emitted by an OTel-instrumented AI service is billed as a custom metric, not as a platform-included metric, even when the metric name follows the standard `gen_ai.*` namespace exactly.

Cardinality control in the metrics layer is implemented through three mechanisms.

The first is tag selection at instrumentation time: the team decides which attributes appear as metric tags and which appear only in span attributes, trace metadata, or separate log stores. The governing principle is that tags should come from bounded vocabularies. Model name has a small, finite value set. Provider name is bounded. Deployment environment, service name, and team are all bounded. Session identifier, request identifier, document identifier, and any substring of prompt or response text are unbounded and must not appear as metric tags. The discipline of applying this rule consistently across a codebase that includes AI-generated instrumentation requires structural enforcement rather than code-review reliance.

The second mechanism is Datadog's **Metrics without Limits** feature, which decouples metric ingestion from metric indexing. A metric can be ingested at full cardinality but indexed only at the tag subset the team specifies. The billing meter applies to indexed time series, not ingested time series. A team that has already emitted high-cardinality metrics can apply a tag configuration that indexes only the bounded dimensions while preserving the full-cardinality data in the ingestion layer for query-time rollup. Metrics without Limits allows this configuration to be applied retroactively, from the platform UI, without code changes, and is reversible. Independent analyses document that applying Metrics without Limits to unqueried metrics can reduce average custom metrics usage by up to 70 percent across accounts that have accumulated cardinality debt (per Datadog Best Practices for Custom Metrics Governance documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[9]</sup>

The third mechanism is the **Cardinality Tags Explorer**, which shows which tag keys are driving a particular metric's cardinality. Teams that have inherited an observability configuration from a previous team, or from an AI-assisted setup process, can use this view to identify the specific tags responsible for cardinality expansion without reading every instrumentation file in the codebase.

In the span layer, cardinality control operates through the distinction between indexed and non-indexed attributes. Every span attribute attached to an LLM span is stored in the trace backend, but only attributes that appear in retention filters and facet configurations are queryable for interactive analysis. A span attribute that carries a session identifier is stored with the span but is not, by itself, a cardinality driver for billing purposes, unlike the equivalent field in a metric tag. The billing driver in the span layer is span volume (the count of indexed spans relative to the per-host inclusion) and span payload size. Prompt text, response text, and retrieved document content should be stored as span payloads rather than as span tags, because payloads are subject to the 1 MB per-span size limit rather than contributing to metric cardinality (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[10]</sup>

The practical consequence is an instrumentation pattern that many production AI teams have converged on independently: metrics carry only bounded dimensions (model, provider, service, environment, span kind), spans carry rich operational attributes (token counts, latency, finish reason, cost estimate), and payloads carry the conversational content. This three-layer separation applies cardinality control at the metric layer, applies sampling discipline in the span layer, and routes the verbose content to the log tier where retention routing can manage its storage cost.

The organizational dimension of cardinality control is addressed by Datadog's **Usage Attribution** feature, which provides a per-team breakdown of custom metric usage and cost. Available on the Enterprise plan, Usage Attribution allows platform engineering teams to hold individual product squads accountable for their observability footprint, converting the abstract constraint of cardinality discipline into a concrete, visible number that each team owns (per Datadog Best Practices for Custom Metrics Governance documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[9]</sup> Teams that have adopted this approach report it as the single most effective forcing function for maintaining cardinality discipline across a growing engineering organization.

## Retention Tiers: Logs and the Routing Decision

Log management is where the cost consequences of AI workloads are most concentrated, and where the retention tier architecture provides the most direct financial relief.

Datadog's log management billing has a two-part structure: ingestion at $0.10 per gigabyte and indexing at $1.70 per million events for 15-day retention or $2.55 per million events for 30-day retention. These two charges stack: a team pays to move the log to Datadog's ingestion layer, and pays again to make it searchable. For traditional application logs, where the typical event is a single structured log line of a few hundred bytes, the indexing cost is modest relative to the value. For AI payload logs, where the typical event is a multi-kilobyte conversational record, the indexing cost scales with the size and verbosity of the model's output, regardless of whether the team is actively querying those payloads on a daily basis (per Datadog APM billing documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[2]</sup>

**Flex Logs**, which reached general availability on August 3, 2023, addresses this by decoupling log storage from log query compute. The Flex Logs tier stores log data at approximately $0.05 per million events per month for storage, with separate compute charges applied only when the team actively queries the stored data. The storage cost is roughly one-thirtieth the indexing cost of the standard 15-day tier, making Flex Logs appropriate for data categories that need to be retained but are queried infrequently: full conversational AI payloads needed only during post-incident quality investigations. Flex Logs reached $50 million ARR in six quarters after launch, which Datadog described as the fastest product ramp in the company's history at the time of the Q4 2024 earnings announcement (per Datadog Q4 2024 earnings transcript, cited in Claude research report, Q2 2026).<sup>[11]</sup>

**Flex Frozen**, announced in 2025, extends the storage tier architecture to retention periods of up to seven years. Flex Frozen uses commodity storage pricing and supports Archive Search, which allows teams to query frozen log data without rehydrating it into the standard index. The architecture enables a compliance use case that traditional Datadog log management could not address cost-effectively: retaining AI conversational logs for extended regulatory retention periods without paying standard indexing rates for the full retention window.<sup>[12]</sup>

The practical routing decision that production AI teams make with these tiers follows a hierarchy of query frequency and urgency:

| Data Category | Destination Tier | Retention | Query Access |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Operational logs, error logs, structured AI span data for real-time incident response | Standard index | 15-day | Immediate |
| AI payload logs, conversation histories, retrieved document records for post-incident quality investigation | Flex Logs | Configurable | On-demand, compute-charged |
| Long-term retention logs for compliance audit or legal review | Flex Frozen | Up to 7 years | Archive Search, compute-charged |

This routing table, applied as a pipeline configuration rather than per-service code change, is the single highest-leverage cost intervention available for AI workloads. The communications company that opened this chapter recovered the majority of its $112,000 annual overrun by moving AI payload logs from the standard index to Flex Logs alone (per Datadog Best Practices for Log Management documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[13]</sup>

For AI workloads specifically, the canonical routing configuration is to capture prompt and response payloads in spans (subject to the 1 MB per-span size limit) for the most recent production traffic where quality investigations are most likely to be needed, and simultaneously log the same payloads to Flex Logs with a longer retention window for cases where the investigation occurs after the span retention period has expired. When the payload exceeds the span ceiling, the recommended pattern from Datadog's own documentation is to store a content hash or a reference identifier in the span metadata field, with the full content in the Flex Logs store indexed under the same reference key. This allows investigators to locate a span by its operational properties (latency, token count, cost, model, session ID) and then retrieve the full conversational content from Flex Logs using the reference identifier (per Datadog LLM Observability documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[10]</sup>

The rehydration pattern for standard log archives differs from the Flex Frozen query pattern. Standard archives write logs to external object storage (Amazon S3, Azure Blob Storage, or Google Cloud Storage) and require a rehydration step to make them queryable in Datadog. Rehydration is a compute operation charged separately from storage, and teams that have not considered rehydration costs when designing their archival policy sometimes encounter a secondary bill shock when they attempt to rehydrate a large archive for a compliance review. Datadog's documentation recommends setting a maximum scan size limit on archives to prevent runaway rehydration costs from an overly broad query against a large archive. Flex Frozen's Archive Search addresses the rehydration cost concern by allowing querying without a rehydration step, at a compute cost that scales with query scope rather than the full archive volume (per Datadog Best Practices for Log Management documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[13]</sup>

## Sampling Configuration in Practice

Sampling is the third axis of cost governance, and it interacts with both cardinality control and retention tiering in ways that require configuration to be coordinated rather than applied independently.

The three sampling strategies available to Datadog users are head-based, tail-based, and adaptive. Head-based sampling makes the keep-or-drop decision at the point where the trace begins, before any downstream span data exists. A head-based sample rate of 10 percent keeps one in ten traces in their entirety and drops the other nine. This reduces indexed span volume linearly with the sampling rate, but it does so without knowing whether the dropped traces would have been interesting: a 10 percent sample will drop 90 percent of error traces if errors are uniformly distributed across the sample space. They are not uniformly distributed in production AI systems, where failures cluster around specific models, specific prompt patterns, and specific infrastructure states.

Tail-based sampling defers the decision until the trace has completed, allowing the sampling logic to examine the full trace before deciding whether to keep it. A tail-based configuration can keep 100 percent of traces containing errors, 100 percent of traces with latency above the 95th percentile, and a representative sample of healthy traces. For AI workloads, where the most diagnostically valuable traces are precisely those with anomalous cost, anomalous latency, or failed evaluations, tail-based sampling captures the signal at substantially reduced volume compared to keeping everything. The reference architecture for AI workloads specifies tail-based sampling as the recommended configuration, with 100 percent retention for errors and guardrail violations and 5 to 10 percent random sampling for healthy operations.<sup>[3]</sup>

A concrete sampling configuration for an agentic workload, applied at the OpenTelemetry Collector layer, implements this hierarchy:

| Sampling Rule | Retention Rate | Rationale |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Traces containing error spans or guardrail violations | 100% | Full diagnostic coverage for every failure |
| Traces with latency above P95 threshold | 100% | Anomalous sessions preserved regardless of outcome |
| Traces with estimated cost above 2x median session cost | 100% | Cost anomalies captured for investigation |
| All remaining healthy traces | 5 to 10% | Representative baseline at controlled volume |

The practical implementation goes through either the Datadog Agent's trace pipeline or the OpenTelemetry Collector's tail-sampling processor. Both approaches buffer the trace until it completes, apply the sampling logic, and emit or drop the complete trace. The buffering requirement means that tail-based sampling introduces a processing delay and a memory overhead relative to head-based sampling. For AI workloads where individual traces can span minutes of execution time with variable depth, the buffer sizing requires explicit configuration to avoid dropping long-running agent sessions due to buffer timeout before the trace completes.

Adaptive sampling, which adjusts sampling rates dynamically based on traffic volume, is relevant for AI workloads where traffic is bursty. During a low-traffic period, a higher sampling rate preserves diagnostic coverage. During a high-traffic period, the same rate would consume the span inclusion too quickly. Datadog's adaptive sampling feature adjusts the rate automatically to maintain indexed span counts within configured limits, trading sampling rate coverage for cost predictability (per Datadog Adaptive Sampling documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[14]</sup> The tradeoff is that during high-traffic spikes, the adaptive sampler reduces coverage precisely when the team may most want full fidelity, because load spikes are often the moments when failures occur.

Environment-based sampling is an additional dimension that teams frequently neglect. Development and staging environments generate spans with the same billing implications as production spans if they are routed to the same Datadog account and index. Several cost analyses recommend applying aggressive sampling or error-only retention in non-production environments, reducing non-production span volume by 80 to 90 percent relative to a full-instrumentation configuration.<sup>[15]</sup> For AI workloads, this recommendation has an additional motivation: development and staging LLM calls often involve verbose test prompts and long-form debugging sessions that generate atypically large spans and logs, which are not representative of production traffic and should not be stored at production retention levels.

## The Billing Cliff at the Agent Boundary

The shift from LLM applications to agentic architectures introduces a step-change in observability cost that deserves direct treatment. A simple LLM completion API call produces one span. A RAG application produces three to ten spans. An autonomous agent handling a multi-step task produces hundreds to thousands of spans per session, because each planning step, tool call, LLM reasoning turn, re-planning loop, and sub-task delegation generates additional spans in the trace hierarchy.

The documented density range is forty to seventy-five spans per user interaction in representative agentic deployments.<sup>[3]</sup> At the high end of that range, an agentic system handling 100,000 user interactions per month generates between 4 million and 7.5 million LLM-related spans monthly from those interactions alone. Against the 1 million indexed spans included per APM host, this workload requires four to seven-and-a-half times the standard per-host inclusion before a single span from any other part of the application is counted. The overage cost at $1.70 per million spans for the excess adds $5.10 to $11.05 per host per month from this one workload category. Scaled across a multi-host deployment and compounded with tool spans, retrieval spans, and embedding spans that consume the general APM budget, the overage structure becomes the dominant variable in the bill.

This is not a billing anomaly. It is a direct consequence of applying span-based billing to a system architecture whose defining property is the multiplication of discrete steps. The span taxonomy is the right abstraction for organizing and navigating the trace; per-span billing, applied without sampling, is the wrong financial model for routine production traffic where the majority of those steps execute correctly.

The practical resolution is to apply the sampling discipline that makes the billing model tractable: keep all error traces, keep all traces that involve anomalous cost, latency, or evaluation scores, and sample the healthy majority. A 10 percent sample of healthy agentic sessions reduces the span volume contribution from healthy traffic by 90 percent while maintaining the full diagnostic record for every failure. The key monitoring adjustment this requires is a shift from monitoring individual span properties to monitoring the health of the sampling configuration itself. A team that applies tail-based sampling at 10 percent for healthy sessions needs a monitor on the fraction of traces being sampled, because a configuration drift that drops the sampling rate further degrades diagnostic coverage in a way that is not visible from the span store alone.

The interaction between span-based billing and agent depth also creates a pricing dynamic that Datadog's documentation acknowledges but does not explicitly model for customers: more capable agents are more expensive to observe at full fidelity. An agent that solves a problem in three planning steps generates fewer spans than one that reasons through ten steps. If both agents reach the same user-visible outcome, their observability costs differ by a factor proportional to their span count. Teams optimizing agent quality by allowing more thorough deliberation should model this cost dimension explicitly. A 20 percent improvement in agent accuracy that requires three times the average reasoning depth will multiply the observability cost by three, a compounding that affects the total cost of intelligence, not just the inference cost.

## The Observability Pipeline as a Cost Control Layer

One architectural response to Datadog's multi-dimensional billing has been the insertion of an observability pipeline between the application instrumentation layer and the Datadog backend. Datadog Observability Pipelines, and the open-source OpenTelemetry Collector that teams frequently use in its place, allow data transformation and routing at ingest time, before data reaches any storage tier.

The pipeline layer is the point at which several cost-engineering interventions can be applied without requiring changes to application code. High-cardinality tag values can be stripped or hashed at the pipeline before the span reaches Datadog, converting an unbounded session identifier into a hashed value useful for correlation but not contributing to cardinality explosion. Log events can be routed to different destinations based on their content: operational logs to the standard index, AI payload logs to Flex Logs, compliance-relevant logs to Flex Frozen or an external archive. Tail-based sampling logic can be applied at the Collector layer using the OpenTelemetry Collector's tail-sampling processor, which buffers the complete trace and applies keep-or-drop decisions based on the trace's outcome properties.

The reference architecture for AI-heavy Datadog deployments explicitly recommends this pipeline approach: deploy an OTel Collector as the first-mile data management layer, apply edge-level tag distillation to strip unbounded dimensions, implement tail-based sampling at the Collector, and use Datadog Observability Pipelines to route different data categories to different storage tiers.<sup>[3]</sup> The argument for the pipeline layer is that it centralizes cost-control logic in a place visible to the platform team without requiring the product squads that own individual services to implement consistent cardinality discipline in their own code. The platform team owns the pipeline configuration; the product squads own the instrumentation. The governance boundary is structural.

The limitation is real: the pipeline operates on data after instrumentation has already run. A tag that should not have been attached to a metric at the application layer can be stripped at the pipeline, but the pipeline cannot add signal that was never collected. A session that was not traced because of overly aggressive head-based sampling at the application level does not appear in the pipeline for tail-based processing. The pipeline is a cost-control mechanism. It is not a substitute for correct instrumentation at the source.

## Where Cost Governance Fails in Practice

The forensic pattern that emerges from documented Datadog cost overruns in AI workloads is consistent enough across cases to support three named failure modes.

The first, and most common, is what amounts to a silent accumulator: a steady, month-over-month increase in Datadog costs that does not cross any configured alert threshold because the increase per month is proportional enough to the previous month's bill that no anomaly detector flags it. This appears when a new AI feature grows its user base while its instrumentation configuration remains unchanged. Each new user adds proportionally more spans, more log events, and more custom metric time series, all of which compound against a billing model that has no natural ceiling. The early-warning signal is the growth rate of custom metric time series relative to the growth rate of active users: if custom metric time series are growing faster than the user count, a high-cardinality tag dimension is growing with the user base and will eventually produce a billing cliff. The communications company that opened this chapter exhibited this pattern in classic form: six months of proportional growth, each month individually unremarkable, aggregate effect substantial.<sup>[1]</sup>

A startup growing from 1,000 to 100,000 users while logging session identifiers as metric tags will increase its custom metric time series count by a factor of 100 even if no other configuration changes. At $0.05 per series per month, 100,000 session-level series represents $5,000 per month from a single metric. That amount grows linearly with continued user acquisition.<sup>[1]</sup>

The second failure mode is specific to teams adopting the OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions and directing the resulting spans to Datadog. Several independent analyses document that when spans carrying `gen_ai.*` attribute namespaces reach Datadog's ingestion layer, the LLM Observability product activates automatically, triggering the LLM Observability billing tier. Teams that did not intend to purchase LLM Observability but adopted the OTel GenAI conventions for cross-platform portability find themselves billed for the product activation without a deliberate commercial decision. The activation threshold, documented in multiple independent analyses as approximately $120 per day in test environments with minimal LLM traffic, means that even a small-scale OTel GenAI instrumentation deployment can generate meaningful charges without the team understanding why (per Respan market map analysis, 2025; AI Tools Atlas review, May 2026; OpenObserve benchmark analysis, December 2025).<sup>[5]</sup>

The early-warning signal for this pattern is a Datadog bill that shows an LLM Observability line item that the team did not explicitly enable. The remediation options are to engage Datadog sales to configure the LLM Observability product deliberately as a purchased SKU, which allows the team to negotiate terms and configure sampling, or to strip the `gen_ai.*` attributes from spans before they reach Datadog. Stripping the attributes defeats the purpose of the OTel GenAI conventions if the team wants the semantic richness they provide, so the more durable solution is to treat LLM Observability activation as a procurement decision that should accompany the decision to adopt GenAI semantic conventions, not as an afterthought.

The third failure mode, the agent depth multiplier, emerges specifically from agentic architectures that deepen without a corresponding adjustment to sampling configuration. As the engineering team improves the agent's capabilities by allowing more reasoning steps, more tool calls, and more iterative refinement, the span count per session grows. If the sampling configuration was set at deployment time based on the original agent's span count and was not subsequently reviewed, the team is now indexing more spans per session than their original configuration assumed. The bill increases not because of more users but because each user interaction has become more expensive to observe. The early-warning signal is a divergence between user-visible interaction count and indexed span count: if indexed spans are growing faster than sessions, session depth is increasing and the sampling configuration is not compensating.

This failure mode is particularly insidious because it is correlated with engineering success. The team is making the agent more capable. The billing increase arrives as a consequence of that success. A team that monitors only user satisfaction metrics and error rates may not notice the span-count drift until the invoice arrives.

## Cost Governance Tooling in the Datadog Platform

Datadog provides several platform-level tools for monitoring and governing observability costs that are relevant to AI workloads specifically.

The Plan and Usage page provides account-level visibility into custom metric consumption, with a breakdown by metric name and the ability to identify the top custom metrics by volume contribution. For AI workloads, this page is the starting point for identifying which AI-derived metrics are driving custom metric overages. The Custom Metrics tab shows the trailing month's billable usage, the usage trend, and the top metric names by contribution percentage. A team that routes token-count metrics, cost-per-call metrics, and latency histograms for AI operations through the `gen_ai.*` namespace will see these metrics appear in the top-N table when their cardinality or volume is high (per Datadog Best Practices for Custom Metrics Governance documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[9]</sup>

The Metrics Volume Management page provides real-time visibility into custom metric volume by metric name, including a facet for unqueried metrics, which shows metrics that are being emitted and billed but not appearing in any dashboard, monitor, notebook, or SLO query. For AI workloads, this facet is particularly valuable because instrumentation is frequently added before the team has built dashboards for the new signals. A metric that was added during initial instrumentation to track retrieval latency but was never incorporated into a production dashboard is a candidate for either elimination or Metrics without Limits configuration.

The estimated usage metrics that Datadog emits as internal metrics allow teams to build alerting on cost signals before the invoice arrives. The metric `datadog.estimated_usage.apm.ingested_spans` tracks ingested span volume in near-real time. The metric `datadog.estimated_usage.apm.indexed_spans` tracks indexed span volume. The equivalent metrics for logs and custom metrics allow teams to build monitors that fire when any dimension approaches its configured budget before the billing period closes (per Datadog Best Practices for Log Management documentation, accessed Q2 2026; Datadog Best Practices for Custom Metrics Governance documentation, accessed Q2 2026).<sup>[9]</sup> These monitors are the operational equivalent of a spend cap at the observability layer: they do not stop the data from flowing, but they create an alert that gives the team an opportunity to investigate before the budget is exhausted.

## Cloud Cost Management and the Token Attribution Gap

Alongside LLM Observability's span-level cost tracking, Datadog introduced LLM provider cost integrations through its Cloud Cost Management product in October 2025. The OpenAI integration connects to the OpenAI Usage and Cost API to surface real-dollar spend down to individual model and project dimensions, displayed alongside cloud compute costs in the same dashboard. The Anthropic integration connects to the Anthropic Usage and Cost Admin API to ingest Claude model spend and visualize it by workspace, model, and token type (per Datadog DASH talk, "Optimize OpenAI Costs with Cloud Cost Management," October 15, 2025; per Datadog DASH talk, "Track Claude Costs in Datadog Cloud Cost Management," October 15, 2025).<sup>[16]</sup>

These integrations address a visibility gap that existed between LLM Observability's span-level attribution and the provider's own billing. LLM Observability attributes cost at the span level, deriving an estimated cost from token counts and the provider's published pricing at the time of the call. The Cloud Cost Management integrations provide the provider-confirmed actuals, which may differ from estimates due to provider pricing changes, volume discounts, cached token rates, and other factors not visible to the instrumentation layer at span-emit time. Running both in parallel allows a team to validate their span-level cost attribution against the provider's confirmed figures, identifying discrepancies that indicate misconfigured cost estimation or billing structures the team had not modeled correctly.

The attribution gap that neither integration fully closes is the per-user-session cost in a shared production system. Provider billing is at the API key or workspace level. LLM Observability span cost estimation is at the span level with whatever dimensions the team attaches as tags. Connecting individual user interactions to provider-confirmed costs requires a cost attribution pipeline that joins span-level session identifiers (carrying estimated costs) against the provider's usage logs (carrying confirmed costs) at a level of granularity that the provider's APIs do not expose directly. This is **Token-Cost Attribution** in its most specific operational form: the information needed to price individual users' consumption accurately exists in two separate systems and is not automatically joined.

For teams building subscription products with AI features, this attribution gap is a pricing risk, not just a monitoring inconvenience. Until per-session cost attribution can be validated against confirmed provider costs, usage-based pricing for AI features rests on cost estimates rather than confirmed figures. The delta between estimate and confirmed may be small in well-configured systems. In misconfigured systems, especially those affected by prompt-caching accounting changes like the ones documented in the Anthropic billing postmortem of April 2026, the delta can be material.<sup>[17]</sup>

## The Narrowing Cost Ratio

A structural observation that appears across multiple independent analyses deserves direct treatment, because it changes the framing of cost engineering from an internal efficiency concern to a product economics question.

In a traditional instrumented application, observability cost is a fraction of compute cost. A service that costs $10,000 per month in cloud compute might spend $500 to $2,000 per month on observability, a ratio of 5 to 20 percent. This ratio has been stable for long enough that it functions as an implicit baseline assumption in infrastructure budgeting.

AI workloads are narrowing this ratio in two directions simultaneously. Token inference prices have fallen sharply as model providers compete on price and as quantization, caching, and smaller specialized models have become viable for production use. Observability costs have risen with span density, log payload size, and cardinality expansion. The combined effect is that inference prices are falling faster than observability costs at current platform pricing; the ratio is narrowing.

Tian Pan documented this narrowing specifically for a customer-support AI system processing 50,000 user messages per day, generating approximately 200,000 LLM invocations and one million spans per day with 400 megabytes of log data. At standard Datadog pricing without sampling or cardinality control, the observability stack for this workload cost between $129 and $5,170 per month depending on the tool selected, with Datadog falling at the higher end of the range. At the illustrative mid-tier inference rates used in the worked example above, that same workload carries roughly $72,000 per month in inference cost, placing observability at 0.2 to 7.2 percent of total system cost. At very low traffic volumes, or where teams have negotiated aggressive inference pricing well below standard rack rates, the ratio narrows further; the structural observation that Tian Pan makes is about the direction of change across the overall market, not a claim that observability universally exceeds inference cost at any specific scale.<sup>[6]</sup>

The 232-span benchmark result described earlier is consistent with this inversion for low-to-moderate traffic workloads. A team running an internal AI assistant with modest traffic might find that the cost of observing the assistant exceeds the cost of the inference that powers it.<sup>[5]</sup>

This inversion is not an argument against observability. It is an argument for cost engineering as a prerequisite for production AI deployments rather than an afterthought addressed after the first invoice arrives. A team that instruments an AI workload without deliberate sampling, retention routing, and cardinality discipline is not choosing observability over cost. It is accepting the worst-case cost of both dimensions: the worst-case billing exposure and a monitoring configuration that, without these disciplines, may still provide inadequate diagnostic coverage because the span store fills with healthy traces that crowd out the error traces the team actually needs.

## The $112,000 Recovery, Revisited

The communications company that began this chapter recovered its cost structure in six weeks. The consultants' report identified three primary contributors to the overrun: first, AI payload logs routed to the standard index at 15-day retention rather than to Flex Logs; second, a token-usage metric carrying a session identifier as a tag, producing unbounded time series that consumed the custom metrics inclusion and generated overages; third, no sampling configuration on the AI feature's spans, so every session was indexed in full including healthy sessions with no diagnostic value.

The fixes were structural, not code-level. The log routing change moved AI payloads from the standard index to Flex Logs, reducing the log cost for that data category by roughly 97 percent at the storage tier while preserving full queryability via Flex Logs compute charges when investigations required it. The metric tag change removed the session identifier dimension and replaced it with a hashed session tier (premium, standard, trial) that bounded the cardinality to three values. The span sampling configuration applied tail-based sampling that kept 100 percent of error traces and 10 percent of healthy traces. Total indexed span count dropped by approximately 87 percent, returning within the per-host inclusion without generating overages.<sup>[1]</sup>

The recovered bill reflected a system that was better configured, not less observable. Error trace retention was higher than before the changes, because previously the unsampled healthy-trace volume had consumed the index capacity that error traces needed. Payload log retention was longer than before the changes, because Flex Logs pricing allowed the team to extend the retention window beyond 15 days at lower cost than the standard index had charged for 15 days. Metric coverage was unaffected, because the session identifier that was removed had never been used in a production dashboard or alert. Cardinality control and retention tiering, applied together, produced more diagnostic coverage for less cost than the undisciplined configuration they replaced.

That outcome is what cost engineering, practiced as a first-class engineering discipline and not as a cost-reduction exercise, looks like in practice.

Observability cost is the first engineering problem that scales with the quality and ambition of the AI system you build.

## Key Points

- Token-cost attribution requires three inputs from separate systems: span token counts, estimated costs, and provider billing actuals; no Datadog product closes this loop automatically.
- The five billing dimensions compound simultaneously under AI workloads; per-pillar cost reviews miss the interaction effects that produce bill-shock events.
- Tail-based sampling at 100 percent error retention and 5 to 10 percent healthy traces reduces indexed span volume by roughly 90 percent.
- Routing AI payload logs to Flex Logs, at one-thirtieth the standard index cost, recovered most of the $112,000 annual overrun in the opening case.
- Agent depth compounds span cost: more capable agents produce more spans per session, and sampling configurations calibrated at deployment drift as reasoning depth grows.
