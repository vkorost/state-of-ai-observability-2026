# How This Book Was Written

This book was not typed. It was assembled by a pipeline of language-model agents running under Claude Code, each with one job, run one at a time, with a coordinator that checked the output of each agent before the next one started. The byline records the division of labor exactly. The agents wrote the prose. The instruction, the source corpus, and the constraints came from a human editor.

The risk in letting a language model write a reference book is specific. A model asked to open a chapter with a vivid scene, handed a corpus that contains patterns rather than ready-made scenes, will construct a scene. Constructing plausible detail is the thing these models do most fluently, and on the page it is indistinguishable from fact. An earlier draft of this book carried exactly that failure: chapter-opening anecdotes that read as documented incidents and were not. The pipeline that produced this edition was rebuilt to prevent it, and the prevention is structural rather than a promise in a preface.

Several mechanisms do the work. The no-invention rule lives inside each Writer agent's own instructions, not only in the front matter, so the agent generating the prose is the agent bound by the rule. Each Writer draws only from assigned source files. After a chapter is written, a Fact-Trace agent reads it against those sources and checks that every specific claim (every date, percentage, dollar figure, company, role, and technical specification) traces to a source. Claims that do not trace are flagged, and the pipeline halts on the flag for an editorial decision rather than papering over it. A Registry tracks the factual claims and named concepts across chapters so that a term means the same thing in Chapter 9 that it meant in Chapter 3. Two reviewers run in sequence, one specialized for source provenance and mechanical compliance, the other for prose and voice, because the question "is this true and sourced" and the question "is this well written" are different questions and a single reviewer answers neither well.

The book also separates what it can assert from what it can only date. A claim about a durable engineering property is stated plainly and carries the weight of a settled fact. A claim about a vendor's current feature coverage, billing model, or roadmap carries a verification tag that fixes it to the quarter it was checked, because that kind of claim expires on the vendor's next release. The reader is meant to feel the difference from the prose, and to re-verify the dated claims without re-litigating the durable ones.

This edition is a revision of an earlier manuscript. The revision preserved every empirical claim and every endnote from the prior version under a gate that halts whenever a citation would be dropped, so that improving the prose could not quietly cost the book its sources. The numbers, the incidents, and the citations are the same facts the earlier edition verified. What changed is how they read.

None of this makes the book infallible. It makes the book auditable. Every empirical claim carries an endnote to its source, and a reader who wants to check the work has the trail to do it.
