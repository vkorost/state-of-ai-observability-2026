# Research — SLOs for Non-Deterministic AI Systems

*File: Research_-_SLOs_for_NonDeterministic_AI_Systems.md*

The SRE community has **not converged on a discipline for quality SLOs and error budgets for non-deterministic AI**. As of May 2026, the prevailing posture across thought leaders (Charity Majors, Phillip Carter, Niall Murphy, Todd Underwood), vendors (Datadog, New Relic, Dynatrace, Langfuse, Braintrust, Arize, WhyLabs, Fiddler), and major operators (Microsoft Azure Copilot, Bloomberg, Shopify, LinkedIn, GoDaddy, Anthropic, Honeycomb) is that **the building blocks exist — wide-event traces, LLM-as-judge scorers, drift detection, burn-rate alert engines — but the opinionated SLO/error-budget *policy* layer is missing**. Where practitioners do gate on quality, they call it **regression testing or release gates**, not error budgets. Only one major-vendor doc (Dynatrace) explicitly uses the phrase "error budget" in an AI context, and it scopes it to performance and cost rather than hallucination. The academic literature treats *latency* SLOs for LLM serving (TTFT, TPOT, goodput) as a mature subject but treats *quality* SLOs as an open problem — with at least two papers arguing hallucination is mathematically inevitable, which makes a zero-error budget structurally impossible. The community has decisively rejected static thresholds for output quality and has settled on a few patterns (wide-event traces, sampled LLM-as-judge, business-outcome SLIs, regression gates on judge scores), but the synthesis into a Google-SRE-style framework remains aspirational.

## SLO candidates that practitioners actually use

The clearest pattern: **practitioners do not propose a single "hallucination SLO." They decompose "correct" into stackable signals across four layers — operational, structural, behavioral-quality, and outcome — and then pick SLIs from layers that can be measured deterministically against the system's specific contract.**

**Operational signals** are universally adopted and well-instrumented. Datadog ships token counts, costs in nanodollars, span/trace error rates, embedding token counts, prompt cache hit/miss costs, and reasoning-token counts as first-class metrics in `ml_obs.*`. New Relic exposes the same operational surface via `LlmChatCompletionMessage`, `LlmEmbedding`, and per-token cost. Dynatrace organizes them under "stability, latency, load, model drift, data drift, cost." Bloomberg's KubeCon talk series (Alexa Griffith, Sal Furino) is the most concrete public example: a multi-cluster GenAI inference platform with explicit SLOs on TTFT, TPOT, token throughput, and queue depth, integrated through Envoy AI Gateway, Prometheus, and KServe. The serving-side research literature (Sarathi-Serve OSDI '24, AdaServe MLSys '25, SCOOT at Ant Group WWW '25) treats **goodput — the fraction of requests served within latency SLO — as the canonical SLI** for inference serving.

**Structural-correctness signals** are the most under-appreciated trick. Phillip Carter's published SLO for Honeycomb's Query Assistant (target 75% initial, raised to 80% over time, seven-day window) defines the SLI as **any failure in {API call, output parse, schema validate, downstream query execution} returning false**. This converts a non-deterministic correctness question into deterministic sub-checks, and lets the team alert on a budget burn from a vendor-style outage as easily as from a model regression. Shopify's Global Catalogue uses **token-probability uncertainty** as a related signal: outputs with low joint log-probability route automatically to human review. LinkedIn constrains its routing LLM to emit YAML and treats schema-validation failures as deterministic error events. The pattern: **whenever you can squeeze the output through a deterministic boundary, do so, and let that boundary carry your SLI**.

**Behavioral-quality signals** are where practice diverges sharply from theory. Datadog's LLM-as-a-Judge templates ship **hallucination, failure-to-answer, topic relevancy, toxicity, sentiment, goal completeness, tool selection, tool argument correctness, and prompt injection** as managed evaluators. Arize Phoenix ships QA Correctness, Hallucination, Faithfulness, Context Relevancy, User Frustration, Citation, and a family of agent-specific evaluators (tool calling, tool selection, parameter extraction, path convergence, planning, reflection). Fiddler's proprietary "Trust Models" expose RAG Faithfulness (binary), Answer Relevance 2.0 (ordinal), Context Relevance, Fast Faithfulness (sub-100ms), and a granular safety taxonomy (harassing, harmful, hateful, illegal, racist, sexist, sexual, unethical, violent). Braintrust ships Factuality, Relevance, and Safety scorers via `autoevals`. Critically, **no vendor publishes opinionated targets for these metrics**. Braintrust comes closest, citing illustrative thresholds — "factuality at 0.85 or higher, relevance at 0.90 or higher, safety at a 100% pass rate" — but explicitly frames them as release criteria, not SLOs.

**Outcome signals** are the position taken by the most senior practitioners. Jay Lees (Meta Senior Staff Production Engineer) at SRECon EMEA 2025: *"For ads, that might be the click-through rate. If the CTR rises, your AI is likely improving the experience; if it falls, something has regressed."* Honeycomb cites Duolingo defining quality as "anything that could put a user's streak at risk." Shopify Sidekick tracks **activation rate** as the production guardrail because judge correlation with human raters is imperfect (Cohen's Kappa 0.61 vs. human baseline of 0.69). The underlying argument, articulated by Sylvain Kalache in *The New Stack*'s SREcon recap: *"The only reliable arbiter of 'correct' is the business outcome: did the assistant resolve the case, did the user convert, did revenue per session hold?"* Outcomes lag, so the consensus is to **pair an outcome SLI with a fast operational/structural SLI** and accept that the model-quality layer in between is a leading indicator, not a contract.

**User-feedback signals** sit between behavioral and outcome layers. New Relic emits `LlmFeedbackMessage` as a first-class event. Honeycomb's Query Assistant captures explicit yes/no/not-sure responses correlated to trace IDs. Brendan Burns (Azure Copilot) at SRECon 2025 confirmed that Microsoft uses thumbs-up/thumbs-down feedback as the primary user-facing signal alongside automated LLM-judge tests. Jessica Kerr (Honeycomb) was candid about the noise: in a live demo, ~29% of users left "mostly no" feedback despite working outputs, and her own assessment was *"I cannot define whether this is good… they just aren't gonna approve anything that gives 'em a high score."* User feedback works as a **lagged, biased signal**, not as a primary SLO.

## The ground-truth problem and what replaces it

Traditional SLOs measure against an oracle. For LLM outputs there is no oracle in production, and the published community response is a stack of approximate techniques applied at different sampling rates and latencies.

**Decompose to deterministic sub-checks first.** This is the cheapest and most reliable move, and Phillip Carter's Query Assistant SLO is its canonical example. Anything the model produces that can be parsed, validated, or executed against a downstream deterministic system carries a real boolean signal. **Schema validation, tool-call argument validation, downstream success rates, and "does this SQL actually run" become the bulk of the SLI surface for any system that has a structured boundary**. Shopify, LinkedIn, GoDaddy, and Honeycomb all rely on this layer as the first line.

**LLM-as-judge in production is now the dominant proxy for behavioral quality, with sampling**. Datadog runs judge templates in near real time and ships results into the SLO engine as countable events. Braintrust's online scoring runs **asynchronously with documented sampling guidance — 1–10% for high-volume applications, 50–100% for low-volume or critical ones — with zero impact on request latency**. Fiddler runs proprietary fine-tuned judges under 100ms to support real-time guardrails. Arize Phoenix and Langfuse offer reference-free judges (Ragas faithfulness/answer-relevancy/context-precision integration in Langfuse, Hallucination/QAEvaluator in Phoenix) that don't require a labeled dataset and can run on production traces. The critical practitioner insight, repeated across Shopify, GoDaddy, Hamel Husain's field guide, and Eugene Yan's eval-process essay: **the judge must itself be calibrated against human raters before being trusted**, with reported correlation or Cohen's Kappa, and the calibration must be re-run as the underlying model drifts. Two 2025–2026 papers (*Rubrics as an Attack Surface*, *Who Judges the Judge?* from Wells Fargo) document concrete drift in judge rubrics — harmlessness accuracy dropping 27.9% under adversarial pressure — so a production LLM-as-judge SLO has to monitor the judge's own reliability.

**Sampled human review** remains the last line. Anterior (healthcare prior-authorization) dynamically routes uncertain cases to human review using LLM-judge confidence. Shopify uses token-probability uncertainty as a routing signal. The Applied-LLMs collective (Eugene Yan, Hamel Husain, Jason Liu, Shreya Shankar, Bryan Bischof, Charles Frye) explicitly recommends "look at samples every day" as part of on-call rotations, citing the **"intern test"** — if a human intern in the relevant domain could grade the output given the same context, the task is fair to evaluate, and a sampled human review pipeline is the ground truth that calibrates everything else.

**Distribution drift as a first-class SLI.** This is the technique most directly transplanted from the pre-LLM ML observability tradition. Maria Vechtomova (Cauchy, ex-Zalando) at SRECon EMEA 2025: *"The statistical properties of the data can change, and your model stops performing. That's what happened during COVID: forecasting and recommender systems broke because we had never seen that kind of data before."* WhyLabs builds its whole product around whylogs-based drift profiles. Arize provides UMAP embedding drift visualizations. Dynatrace categorizes "model drift" and "data drift" as named observability concerns. Charity Majors frames it as **"representativity drift"** in her 2023 *LLMs Demand Observability-Driven Development* post: *"evaluate the representativity of your production data set… consider your dataset for representativity drift."* In practice, drift detection alerts on **the absence of evidence rather than the presence of failures**, which fits the hallucination ground-truth problem well.

**Service drift** — the documented phenomenon that hosted LLMs change behavior under the same prompt due to silent provider-side updates — is now a recognized SLI of its own (PMC12863567, 2025; longitudinal LLM-judge benchmarks). Practitioners pin model versions and treat unannounced behavior shifts as an alert condition.

## What "error budget" means when correctness is probabilistic

The community has not solved this, and several thinkers argue it is not solvable in the original Google-SRE sense.

**The structural argument against a clean answer.** Two 2024 papers (*LLMs Will Always Hallucinate*, Banerjee et al. arXiv:2409.05746; *Hallucination is Inevitable*, Xu et al. arXiv:2401.11817) argue from undecidability and learning theory that the hallucination rate **cannot be driven to zero by construction**. Any quality error budget for an LLM is therefore necessarily non-zero, and the question becomes how to set the rate, not how to spend down toward zero. Applied-LLMs reports the practitioner-observed floor: **"5–10% baseline rate, challenging to get below 2%, even on simple tasks like summarization,"** citing LLM providers.

**The implicit budget in real published SLOs is huge.** Honeycomb's Query Assistant target of **75% initially, 80% after fixes — versus the 95–99.9% targets typical of web services** — is the most explicit published acknowledgment that LLM features ship with budgets one to two orders of magnitude larger than traditional services. Phillip Carter is blunt: *"Most services have a 95% or higher success target, but we felt that didn't make sense for the initial release of a feature using an LLM."* His operational rule of thumb: *"Start with 50% or higher as your success rate, but don't go higher than 90%."*

**Reframing "uptime" as "quality."** Denys Vasyliev's *AI Reliability Engineering: Welcome to the Third Age of SRE* (The New Stack, June 2025) is the most pointed reframing: *"Unlike crashing pods or failing endpoints, silent model degradation slips under the radar — the model keeps responding, but its answers grow weaker, biased, or just wrong. Users don't see 500 errors; they get hallucinations, toxic outputs, or faulty decisions. In the world of AI, correctness is uptime. When reliability means quality, degradation is downtime."* Under this reframe an error-budget burn is triggered by accuracy or groundedness regression, not just availability. Sylvain Kalache's SRECon EMEA recap summarized the consensus among Microsoft's Brendan Burns, Anthropic's Todd Underwood, Meta's Jay Lees, and Stanza's Niall Murphy: *"Many LLM demos have hit the first nine — they work about 90% of the time — but there are many more nines to conquer."* The point is explicitly that the discipline is unsettled.

**Where vendors mention error budgets at all.** Only Dynatrace's AI Observability docs use the phrase in the AI context: *"Monitor service quality and cost: Implement error budgets for performance and cost control. Validate model consumption and response times. Prevent quality degradation by monitoring models and usage patterns in real time."* This scopes the budget to **performance and cost, not hallucination**. Datadog's error-budget and burn-rate-alert engines are general-purpose; nothing in their LLM Observability docs templates them for quality metrics. New Relic SLM and Honeycomb SLOs are similarly capability-not-recipe.

**The closest live practitioner analogue is a regression budget**, not an availability budget. GoDaddy publishes the explicit policy: **"Set up automatic merge blocking when performance regresses more than 5% compared to baseline metrics."** Braintrust documents the same shape as a CI/CD release pattern: *"Teams set minimum quality thresholds for each metric… and define regression tolerance, meaning no metric can fall below the current production baseline by more than a set percentage. Alerts fire when evaluation scores drop, not just when latency spikes."* These are deploy-time gates, not runtime burn-rate policies. Hamel Husain articulates the philosophical shift cleanly: *"Unlike traditional unit tests, you don't necessarily need a 100% pass rate. Your pass rate is a product decision."*

**HIP-LLM** (arXiv:2511.00527, Nov 2025) is the most theoretically ambitious academic attempt: it defines LLM reliability as *"probability of failure-free operation over N future tasks under a given Operational Profile"* and computes a posterior reliability envelope — effectively a probabilistic error-budget formulation. It has not been adopted in industry blogs.

The fair summary: **the community recognizes the error-budget question, agrees the budget must be much larger than for traditional services, agrees the budget must account for silent decay rather than visible failures, and has produced essentially no published policies that look like Google's multi-window burn-rate alerts applied to a hallucination rate.**

## Alerting when the output distribution is the data

The community has unanimously rejected static threshold alerts on quality signals. The actual replacement stack:

**Burn-rate alerts on multi-window SLOs over decomposed booleans.** Phillip Carter's pattern at Honeycomb: a seven-day SLO window, with **a Slack notification four hours before budget exhaustion, and an explicit anti-pattern against PagerDuty for LLM features** because *"You can have a rogue user who intentionally tries to get your feature to mess up through prompt injection… don't let their nonsense cause someone to get woken up in the middle of the night."* Austin Parker (Honeycomb, April 2026) extends this to agentic systems: *"If an agent is making code changes that technically pass tests but cause a gradual SLO burn, you need that burn rate alert to fire and trigger a review. The agent doesn't need to know it's drifting — your observability platform does."*

**High-cardinality breakdown over wide events instead of pre-aggregated thresholds.** This is Charity Majors' core position. From her March 2026 post on agent observability: *"If you are trying to do anomaly detection with no a priori knowledge of what to look for, the anomaly has to be fairly large to be detected. But if you do have some knowledge of intent, along with precision tooling, these anomalies can be tracked and validated even when they are exquisitely minute."* The mechanism is **wide structured events with user, prompt-template, model-version, tool-call, and retrieval-source as dimensions**, so a drop in groundedness for one tenant or one prompt variant is detectable even when global rates are stable.

**Anomaly detection on quality scores.** Datadog's LLM Observability Insights performs outlier detection over a one-week lookback on its OOTB evaluator scores. Dynatrace Davis AI applies seasonal and forecasted baselines via Site Reliability Guardian's **auto-adaptive thresholds**, which Dynatrace's own data-science team uses to gate Davis AI model regressions. Arize provides drift detection over embedding distributions. These features remain rare and per-vendor; no standard pattern has emerged.

**Continuous shadow evaluation and "AI circuit breakers."** Vasyliev proposes automated rollback to a stable model version when an SLO is breached. Microsoft's Azure Copilot team described continuous shadow evaluation pre-deployment with **LLM-generated tens of thousands of test cases plus human thumbs feedback** as the deploy gate. Bloomberg's Envoy AI Gateway architecture supports dynamic model-based routing that can shift traffic away from a degrading model in real time.

**Score smoothing and retries on judge-flagged failures.** Practitioners cited in Monte Carlo's writeup auto-rerun evals on flagged low scores because *"one in every ten tests spits out absolute garbage."* This is a recognition that the judge itself is non-deterministic and that a single low score is not an alert condition; smoothing windows or quorum-of-judges (the Wells Fargo "LLM Jury-on-Demand" paper, arXiv:2512.01786) are emerging answers.

**No published Bayesian framework specifically for LLM SLOs.** The serving-side literature has plenty (BO in SCOOT, queueing models in QLM), but on the output-quality side this is a gap. Microsoft's empirical incident study (arXiv:2511.07424, Nov 2025) is informative on the operational side — **74% of 156 high-severity LLM inference incidents were auto-detected from infrastructure signals; ~60% were inference-engine failures; ~28% required hotfixes** — but does not propose a probabilistic alerting framework for output quality.

## Who owns AI SLOs

The most consistent pattern across published case studies is **hybrid ownership where product or domain experts define what "good" means, ML engineering operationalizes the judge, and SRE owns the burn-rate alert and trace infrastructure**.

**Domain experts and PMs own the rubric.** Hamel Husain's field guide is the strongest articulation: *"Empower domain experts to write prompts and define eval criteria."* Phillip Carter, a Principal PM at Honeycomb, wrote the Query Assistant prompts, designed the user-feedback schema, and calibrated the LLM judge — reaching **>90% agreement over three iterations**. Shopify Sidekick is the same pattern at larger scale: domain experts label conversations to seed the Ground Truth Set; ML engineers build judges; product owns the activation-rate guardrail. Applied-LLMs is explicit that the "AI Engineer alone" anti-pattern fails because criteria drift demands continuous human judgment, citing Shankar et al.: *"It is impossible to completely determine evaluation criteria prior to human judging of LLM outputs."*

**ML / AI engineering owns the judge implementation and the eval infrastructure.** GoDaddy describes a central AI/ML team operating an "LLM Evaluation Workbench" that hands gating tools to feature teams. Anthropic, per Tom Brown's January 2024 remarks, has *"a team that's fully focused on bringing that [hallucination] number down"* — the frontier-lab pattern of a dedicated reliability/safety org.

**Platform engineering owns the context-assembly SLOs.** This is a newer pattern emerging from platformengineering.org and CNCF talks: retrieval freshness, embedding-index health, PII filtering, and rate-limited tool wrappers are platform-team SLOs because they are shared across all agent developers. Bloomberg's KubeCon talks position the AI Gateway (Envoy-based) as the platform-engineering surface, with **token-level rate limiting, distributed inference routing, and observability hooks** as platform SLO concerns.

**SRE owns the burn-rate alerting and the agent guardrail.** Austin Parker at Honeycomb describes a "trust ramp" model: *"The innermost ring is changes where the agent can self-verify: it makes a change, runs the tests, checks the SLOs, confirms that the metrics didn't regress. If all of that passes, the change can be auto-merged, but only for categories of changes where you've already validated work this way."* SRE owns the guardrail; agents and ML engineers operate inside it. Denys Vasyliev's prescriptive position is that SRE itself must evolve into "AI Reliability Engineering" and own the AI Gateway as the new control plane. The SREcon EMEA panel (Burns, Underwood, Lees, Vechtomova, Saucedo) leaned cross-functional: **service owners define outcome-level SLIs because only they understand the business metric; SREs provide the burn-rate and observability primitives**.

**No vendor publishes a RACI matrix.** Dynatrace uses entity-ownership tags to auto-route SRG alerts; Datadog has Teams and notification rules tied to `service`/`team`/`ml_app` tags; everyone else leaves it to the customer. **No engineering blog reports a formal "AI SRE" job function as the owner of AI quality SLOs**; the title appears mostly in vendor marketing.

## Open questions and gaps

**Hallucination-rate SLO targets remain unpublished**. No source cited in this research publishes a quantitative industry-consensus target for a hallucination or groundedness rate at the SLO level. Vendor docs ship the metric; nobody ships the target. The closest signals are practitioner anecdotes (5–10% baseline, 2% as a hard floor) and illustrative thresholds in Braintrust docs (factuality ≥ 0.85). This is the single biggest gap in the literature.

**Error budgets applied to quality, not just availability, are aspirational.** Outside Dynatrace's one-sentence mention scoped to performance and cost, and GoDaddy's 5% regression-block policy, no published case study describes a runtime burn-rate alert applied to a quality score. The CI/CD release-gate pattern (Braintrust, GoDaddy) is real and reported; the runtime SRE pattern is not.

**LLM-judge calibration and drift management is an unsolved meta-problem.** Rubric drift (arXiv:2602.13576), judge bias against human raters (PMC12863567), and the fact that judges hallucinate roughly one in ten responses (Monte Carlo's practitioner quote) all mean that a quality SLO built on a single judge is fragile. Quorum approaches (Wells Fargo's LLM Jury) and human-anchored calibration are emerging but not standardized.

**Determinism itself is contested at the infrastructure level.** Hacker News discussion threads and the LLM-42 paper note that floating-point non-associativity and dynamic batching introduce up to 15% variation across nominally deterministic runs. SLO design has not absorbed this; it implies the noise floor of any SLI is larger than naive thresholds assume.

**Organizational ownership has no settled pattern.** The hybrid model described above is the median, but published case studies range from frontier-lab "dedicated reliability team" (Anthropic) to PM-led (Honeycomb) to platform-engineering-led (Bloomberg) to vendor-managed (GoDaddy's workbench). No source argues for a single canonical structure, and the "AIRe" rebrand has not been adopted in job titles in any reported case.

**Agentic systems remain less developed than single-turn LLM apps.** Austin Parker's April 2026 Honeycomb post and Charity Majors' March 2026 agent post are the leading edge of public thinking. The published patterns — instrument the wrapper not the model, treat tool calls and prompt construction as spans, use burn-rate alerts as the "silent drift" detector — are credible but unproven at scale in publicly reported case studies.

**ACM Queue, Google SRE, MLSys, and the Pragmatic Engineer all have null findings on opinionated quality-SLO frameworks.** Google's published GenAI-and-SRE content covers GenAI for SRE workflows (Duet AI, SLOgpt for traditional services) but no canonical "SLOs for GenAI" chapter exists analogous to the SRE Workbook. The de facto reference text remains the 2022 O'Reilly book *Reliable Machine Learning* (Murphy, Underwood, Pelisse, Beyer, Jones), which pre-dates the generative wave and reads as foundational rather than current.

## Conclusion

A practitioner standing up SLOs for a non-deterministic AI service today is on their own with respect to policy and well-supported with respect to instrumentation. The defensible pattern, drawn from the most concrete published examples (Honeycomb Query Assistant, Shopify Sidekick, Microsoft Azure Copilot, Bloomberg AI Platform, GoDaddy), looks like this: instrument the wrapper with wide events; decompose "correct" into stackable deterministic sub-checks at every available boundary; layer sampled LLM-as-judge for behavioral signals with the judge itself calibrated against humans and re-validated for drift; pair every quality SLI with a slower business-outcome SLI as the ultimate arbiter; set initial budgets one to two orders of magnitude looser than traditional services; use burn-rate alerts only on the deterministic-sub-check layer, and use distribution-drift and anomaly detection over wide-event breakdowns for the behavioral layer; and let domain experts own the rubric, ML engineering own the judge, platform engineering own the context-assembly, and SRE own the burn-rate guardrail. The deeper insight is that the SRE discipline is being reshaped by the encounter with non-determinism more than it is reshaping AI: **correctness is becoming a continuous, sampled, statistical property rather than a contractual one, and the published frontier of the field is the slow translation of error-budget thinking from "did the request return 200" to "did the answer stay grounded enough, often enough, for the user to keep using the feature."**
